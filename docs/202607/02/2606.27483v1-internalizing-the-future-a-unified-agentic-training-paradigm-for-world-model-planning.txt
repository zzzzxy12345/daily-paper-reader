# Internalizing the Future: A Unified Agentic Training Paradigm for World Model Planning

Large language model (LLM) agents have demonstrated strong capability in sequential decision-making, yet they remains fundamentally reactive in long-horizon tasks. Unlike humans who employ "what-if" reasoning to evaluate potential plans before commitment, standard agents lack an internal world model to simulate future outcomes. Therefore, we propose to internalize future-aware planning by training a single autoregressive model to verbalize both a prospective state rollout and a plan-conditioned success estimate—a textual analogue of the Q-value. Crucially, we identify a format-capability gap: simply fine-tuning agents on look-ahead traces during post-training leads to superficial mimicry of foresight without genuine predictive grounding. To bridge this gap, we introduce a three-stage training paradigm: (i) World Model Agentic Mid-Training (WM-AMT) to inject latent predictive capabilities into the policy; (ii) Format-Eliciting SFT (FE-SFT) to structure this injected capability; and (iii) Foresight-Conditioned Reinforcement Learning (FC-RL) to refine the calibration and utility of the generated simulations. Evaluated on search and mathematical reasoning tasks, our approach consistently outperforms other training baselines. Our results demonstrate that effective internal world modeling in LLM agents requires a capability-first training pipeline to achieve grounded and calibrated foresight.

# 1 Introduction 

LLM agents have become strong controllers for multi-step tasks given their comprehensive pre-trained semantic knowledge and remarkable capabilities in sequential decision-making [ 1, 2]. However, the pre-vailing paradigm in long-horizon settings remains fundamentally reactive, yet human planning is rarely reactive. Before acting, people often ask a what-if question: if I follow this plan, what is likely to happen next, and how likely is it to succeed? This style of reasoning is central to intelligent behavior because it allows alternatives to be mentally compared before commitment, as shown in Figure 1. In Craik’s classic formulation, effective intelligence depends on carrying a “small-scale model” of external reality that lets possible actions be tried out in thought before they are executed [ 3 ]. Inspired by this pattern of planning, we do not claim full counterfactual reasoning; rather, we target a plan-conditioned form of future simulation and evaluation within a language agent. While standard autoregressive reasoning methods such as Chain-of-Thought [ 4] mainly improve decomposi-tion of the current problem, the ability that we aim to inject is closely related to an internal world model [5, 6 ]. Instead of utilizing separate modules to serve as external world models [ 7 , 8 , 9], our key claim is that, for language agents, this kind of future-aware planning signal should be internalized as part of the policy itself. Moreover, unlike Dreamer-style model-based RL [ 10 , 11 , 12 ], our goal is not to introduce a separate simulator or a dedicated value head. Instead, we train a single autoregressive model not only to serve as 1

This textual estimate is best understood as a verbalized analogue of Q(s, a) or local action value, represented in the same token space as the policy rather than as a claim of full what-if reasoning. Unlike other methods that only train agents to predict future states or transition dynamics [ 13 , 14 , 15 ], our design of internal world modeling is more comprehensive and innovative. Crucially, our verbalized Q-values are designed to be continuously updated and corrected by actual environmental feedback obtained from real execution during RL training, which is also distinct from previous works [16]. Such explicit verbalization offers a transparent reasoning signal, allowing the agent to effectively prune suboptimal paths through implicit deliberation. To inject this capability, a natural idea is to leverage the inherent world modeling potential of LLMs [ 17 ] by training the agent to imagine the future, evaluate it, and then act. However, our preliminary experiments show that this strategy fails when applied only during post-training. With SFT and RL on formatted look-ahead traces, the agent learns the surface form of a simulation but not the predictive competence behind it. Experimental results for SFT and RL are shown in Table 7, and an example is shown in Appendix E.1. We call this failure mode the format-capability gap :the agent can produce a plausible-looking foresight block while the content remains weakly grounded, miscalibrated, or outright misleading, thus introducing noise rather than useful guidance. This motivates us to propose a unified three-stage training paradigm: World Model Agentic Mid-Training (WM-AMT) , Format-Eliciting SFT (FE-SFT) , and Foresight-Conditioned Reinforcement Learning (FC-RL) .WM-AMT injects the underlying predict-then-act capability by augmenting large-scale agentic trajectories with future-aware experience summaries. FE-SFT then teaches the model when and how to externalize this latent capability in a structured foresight block. FC-RL finally improves both action quality and foresight quality by rewarding task success, grounded anticipation, and calibrated confidence. All three stages are implemented within one causal language model, with no additional value head. We evaluate this training paradigm on two representative agentic domains: search and mathematical reasoning. Across both settings, our method consistently outperforms other training baselines. The results show that future-aware behavior in LLM agents is not well served by format supervision alone; it benefits from a training pipeline that first builds the capability, then elicits it, and finally optimizes it under reward. 2World Model Agentic Training 

Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We identify the format-capability gap: post-training on look-ahead formats alone teaches LLM agents to mimic the structure of foresight without reliably acquiring grounded future prediction. • We propose a unified three-stage paradigm, consisting of WM-AMT, FE-SFT, and FC-RL, which internalizes world-model planning inside a single autoregressive policy. Our method verbalizes both future rollout and a plan-conditioned success estimate without introducing an explicit value head, and this verbalized Q-like signal is explicitly trained and refined throughout the pipeline. • We show on search and mathematical reasoning that this capability-first pipeline yields consistent gains over other training baselines. 

# 2 Preliminaries 

# 2.1 World models in LLM agents 

We formulate LLM agents’ decision-making as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) [ 18 ], denoted by M =

⟨S , A, P, R, γ⟩. State st ∈ S includes the initial instruction, historical trajectory, and observations. Action at ∈A represents discrete operations, e.g., text generation or tool invocation. Transition dynamics P (st+1|st, at)

and reward R(st, at) govern environmental feedback. The objective is to learn a parameterized policy 

πθ (at|st) that maximizes the expected return discounted by γ ∈ [0, 1 ]. World models traditionally predict transition dynamics ˆP (st+1|st, at) and rewards ˆR(st, at) [5 ]. However, applying token-level simulation to dense, low-level environmental feedback is computationally intractable. Autoregressively predicting fine-grained future trajectories τf uture = ⟨st+1, at+1, ot+1, . . . , st+k⟩ may waste computational capacity on syntax rather than core causal dynamics, thus causing severe compounding errors [ 19 ]. Thus, we reconceptualize the World Model for LLM agents as an internal semantic simulator . It generates a condensed experience summary of anticipated consequences, defined as ˆzt = Ψ(τf uture ). Here, Ψ is a semantic compression function mapping raw rollouts into high-level predictive foresight. This empowers the agent to perform look-ahead planning ,transitioning from a reactive policy πθ (at|st) to a foresight-conditioned policy πθ (at|st, ˆzt). Ultimately, this enables the agent to evaluate consequences and prune suboptimal trajectories in the latent space before executing real-world actions. 

# 2.2 Mid-training in large language models 

To obtain strong initial policies in terms of both performance and exploration diversity, mid-training [ 20 , 21 ]has become an essential step between general pre-training and post-training, which aims to adapt the LLM’s underlying data distribution toward the target domain. Recent theoretical analyses demonstrate that an effective mid-training stage not only establishes strong policy priors by efficiently pruning the decision space, but also allows the subsequent RL phase to effectively jump across multiple steps during Bellman backups, thus accelerating convergence speed and minimizing post-training regret [ 22 ]. By immersing the LLM in high-density, high-quality specialized sequences, mid-training provides a latent semantic foundation and contextually grounded initialization for post-training. 3World Model Agentic Training 

# 2.3 Group-based RL 

Unlike traditional Actor-Critic methods (e.g., PPO [ 23 ]) that rely on a parameterized value network Vϕ(s) to estimate advantages, group-based RL approaches [ 24 , 25 , 26 ] leverage the statistical distribution of rewards within a sampled batch to approximate the baseline. For a given task prompt x, the agent samples a group of G trajectories {τ1, τ2, . . . , τG } from the current policy πθold . Each trajectory τi represents a full episode of interactions and is assigned a scalar reward R(τi) reflecting the task success. The advantage estimation A(τi)

is derived from the normalized reward in each group: A(τi) = R(τi )−μ({R(τj )}Gj=1)

# 3 Methodology 

# 3.1 The format-capability gap in post-training 

A natural idea to internalize world model into the LLM policy is training the LLM with SFT data that explicitly includes anticipated futures, and then followed by RL. However, our preliminary experiments on the model mid-trained with routine agentic trajectories (see Appendix D.1 and E.1) reveal a format-capability gap. While the agent learns the format of look-ahead reasoning, it fails to simulate accurate future dynamics, often resulting in hallucinations. We attribute it to the inherent nature of post-training: while SFT and RL excel at eliciting latent capabilities, they struggle to inject fundamentally new ones. Without a prior understanding of future consequences, enforcing a look-ahead format merely triggers hallucinated pattern matching rather than genuine future simulation. It motivates us to intervene earlier in the training pipeline via mid-training to construct a foundational data distribution that intrinsically models the future. We propose a three-stage training paradigm to close the format-capability gap, as shown in Figure 2. Given an LLM policy, our goal is not only to produce a well-formed look-ahead trace, but also to internalize transition-aware foresight that improves policy capability. The paradigm consists of: (i) World Model Agentic Mid-Training (WM-AMT) for policy prior formation, (ii) Format-Eliciting Supervised Fine-Tuning (FE-SFT) for structured elicitation, and (iii) Foresight-Conditioned Reinforcement Learning (FC-RL) for reward-aligned policy optimization.

# 3.3 Format-eliciting supervised fine-tuning 

While WM-AMT successfully injects latent look-ahead capabilities into LLM agents, the base policy lacks the structural schema to autonomously and reliably invoke this predictive capability during inference. The FE-SFT stage bridges this gap by aligning the generated trajectory with a predefined, explicit look-ahead reasoning structure. We curate a filtered task-specific instruction-tuning dataset DFE-SFT . Given a current observation st, the policy is trained to project its internal world model into a textual representation of anticipated future states and estimated Q-values, i.e., world model block ˆzt. While adopting a similar generation pipeline for these instances, FE-SFT strictly encloses ˆzt within distinct structural delimiters. Following ˆzt, the policy generates the corresponding executable action at. Since WM-AMT has established a robust causal prior via a large-scale general agentic trajectories, FE-SFT avoids the hallucinations typical of world modeling from scratch. We also use the standard autoregressive negative log-likelihood objective to optimize the agent on DFE-SFT .5World Model Agentic Training

# 4 Related Work 

# 4.1 World models in autonomous agents 

World models are core to model-based RL, where agents learn transition and reward for look-ahead control [5, 28 ]. Classical lines focus on latent-dynamics learning in visually observed environments [ 6, 10 , 11 , 12 ] and more recently, embodied agent for future action-conditioned simulation [29]. It enables models to improve sample efficiency in a latent space or simulate outcomes without real-world interaction. The idea has shifted from pixel-space simulation to language-mediated future prediction and planning due to the rise of LLMs [ 16 , 7]; it also extends to code-generation settings, where world modeling is used to study long-horizon program synthesis and repair [ 14 ]. Despite these advancements, most existing systems treat the world model as an explicit simulator or external module [ 8, 9 ]. For example, WebDreamer [ 9 ] and WebEvolver [ 30 ]construct explicit world models to simulate the environmental feedback, which improve deliberation quality but may introduce additional inference overhead and possible simulator-policy mismatch. In parallel, recent implicit world modeling strategies suggest that early experiences can improve downstream agent behavior without relying on another simulator [ 13 , 15 ]. Extending this implicit world modeling line, our work targets a unified agentic training pipeline for world model planning. 

# 4.2 Agentic data synthesis and mid-training 

The scarcity of expert-level demonstrations has motivated the synthetic data for training LLM agents [ 15 ]. Early imitation-based distillation from stronger teachers [ 31 , 32 ] aids initial alignment but suffers from limited diversity and weak on-policy coverage. Subsequent works, such as AgentSynth [ 33 ] and SCA [ 34 ], broaden task synthesis to expand the exploration space for agentic RL. Another direction shifts from trajectory synthesis to environment synthesis, e.g., classical world-model methods in Section 4.1, which uses learned environments to generate scalable interaction data. However, translating these synthetic interactions into reusable decision-making capabilities necessitates a dedicated training phase. Consequently, mid-training 

[ 20 ] is increasingly adopted to bridge general pre-training and task-level alignment [ 21 , 22 ]. Unlike SFT, mid-training scales to vastly larger, diverse corpora of expert rollouts, such as human demonstrations in device control, tool-use records and code writing [ 35 , 36 , 37 ]. Our perspective is to synthesize agentic data with compressed future experience summary, and internalize a robust predict-then-act policy prior via mid-training.

# 5.1 Experiment setup 

We select an intermediate checkpoint of Youtu-LLM-2B [ 38 ] as our foundation model, and refer to it as Youtu-LLM-2B-Init. This selection is motivated by two practical considerations: (1) the Youtu-LLM family inherently possesses strong native agentic capabilities; (2) a lightweight 2B scale makes our extensive mid-training computationally feasible. To validate the effectiveness of our method, we initialize from the Youtu-LLM-2B-Init immediately following its general pre-training and compare two mid-training variants: (1) Youtu-LLM-2B-Base is trained with standard agentic mid-training on 200B tokens of high-quality agentic trajectory data without changing its structure; (2) Youtu-LLM-2B-Base (WM-AMT, ours) augments the same agentic trajectory data by inserting an explicit world model block into a random split of multi-turn interaction trajectories, which provides supervised signals for future-state prediction. We conduct experiments on two representative agentic tasks: search and mathematical reasoning. 

Search Following Jin et al. [39] , we evaluate our training paradigm on 7 search-augmented QA tasks, including single-hop QA datasets (NQ [ 40 ], TriviaQA [ 41 ], and PopQA [ 42 ]) and multi-hop QA datasets (HotpotQA [ 43 ], 2Wiki [ 44 ], MuSiQue [ 45 ], and Bamboogle [ 46 ]). For retrieval, we use the 2018 Wikipedia dump [ 47 ] as the knowledge source and E5 [ 48 ] as the retriever, and set the number of retrieved passages to 3 across all retrieval-based methods. During evaluation, the results are obtained via greedy sampling, and the metrics are computed via LLM-as-a-judge using DeepSeek V3.1 [49]. 

Mathematical reasoning Following Feng et al. [50] , we evaluate our paradigm on AIME2024/2025/2026 [51 ]. We repeat the evaluation set AIME2024&2025&2026 30 times and report both mean@30 and pass@30. The evaluation hyperparameters of evaluation are set to temperature 1.0 and top-p 0.6.

# 6 Conclusion 

We address the limitations of reactive LLM agents by internalizing future-aware planning into the policy. We identify the format-capability gap , wherein post-training on look-ahead formats merely teaches structural mimicry instead of grounded prediction. To bridge this gap, we propose a unified three-stage training paradigm consisting of WM-AMT, FE-SFT and FC-RL. Evaluations across search and mathematical reasoning show the superiority of our approach over other training baselines. Our results underscore that effective 10 World Model Agentic Training 

internal world modeling in LLM agents demands a capability-first paradigm to achieve truly grounded and calibrated foresight.

# D.5 Analysis of the RL-only baseline and the necessity of SFT 

To address the concern regarding whether success-driven RL alone is sufficient, we introduce and evaluate the RL only baseline across both the standard base model and our mid-trained architecture. As summarized in Table 12, directly applying RL (GRPO) optimization without a preparatory SFT phase results in severe performance degradation in the search task. Crucially, our results in Figure 3 reveals a severe optimization pathology during the RL-only training loop on tool-integrated search tasks. Without a preliminary SFT phase to anchor the structural tool-calling syntax and reasoning schema, the model suffers from an alignment collapse in the later stages of training. Specifically, the agent completely forgets how to interact with external search tools. Driven by the sparse success rewards, the policy prematurely converges on a degenerative local optimum: it collapses into a naive single-turn generation format, directly outputing what it speculates to be the answer while entirely bypassing the necessary multi-hop tool invocation sequences. This finding firmly demonstrates that success-driven RL alone cannot robustly bootstrap complex, structured action sequences from scratch. Consequently, a warm-start SFT phase (such as standard SFT or our proposed FE-SFT) is crucial to establish a s structural prior before exposing the agent to RL.
