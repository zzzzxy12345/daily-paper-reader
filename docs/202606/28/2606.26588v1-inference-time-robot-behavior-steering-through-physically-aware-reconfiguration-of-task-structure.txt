Abstract: A central challenge in deploying learned robot policies is inference-time behavior steering : redirecting a policy at test time to satisfy user preferences not anticipated during training, without retraining. Existing methods fail in two modes: end-to-end methods require fine-tuning or expert-level guidance, while neuro-symbolic methods rely on predefined symbols whose edits can result in logically reasonable but physically infeasible plans. To address this challenge, we propose ReStruct , which builds upon a neural automaton policy that decomposes a visuomotor policy into a high-level state-machine skeleton capturing task struc-ture and a low-level continuous controller represented as a residual policy. Specifi-cally, ReStruct adopts the automaton to represent the preference and incorporates it into the skeleton through a synchronous product, thereby reconfiguring the task structure. With the controller kept frozen, the action priors provided by the skele-ton are updated accordingly to enable physically-aware control under a modified task structure. Extensive experiments from simulation and real-world show that 

ReStruct steers a wide range of preferences, from object-centric specifications to temporal-logic constraints, and after steering surpasses existing methods, exceed-ing VLA models in both task success and preference-following by up to 25%. 

Keywords: Behavior Steering, Neuro-Symbolic Learning, Robot Manipulation

# 1 Introduction 

Recent advances in robot learning have produced policies capable of diverse long-horizon manipula-tion [1, 2]. A central deployment challenge is adapting these policies to user preferences, constraints, or safety requirements not anticipated during training. We refer to it as inference-time behavior steering [3, 4], where policies must be steered at test time without retraining or additional data. Ex-isting approaches generally fail in two modes: end-to-end methods, such as vision-language-action 

> arXiv:2606.26588v1 [cs.RO] 25 Jun 2026

models [5, 6] and diffusion policies [7], adapt behavior either through fine-tuning or imposed guid-ance mechanisms, which are limited to preferences that are approximately differentiable and require expert knowledge. Neuro-symbolic methods [8, 9] support semantic-level preference specification through a symbolic layer, yet isolated symbolic modification may produce plans that are logically valid but physically infeasible, even for preferences expressible within the predefined symbol space. At a conceptual level, both families essentially seek an intermediate representation (e.g., a soft La-grangian in diffusion policies, predicates in neuro-symbolic methods) that translates semantic prefer-ences into adjustments of the continuous policy distribution [4]. We argue that robust inference-time steering requires this representation to be physically-aware : it should ground each preference in the subset of controller-executable behaviors that satisfy the preference. Existing methods fail this prop-erty in opposite ways (Fig. 1). First , end-to-end policies lack an explicit abstraction, so they must re-ground new preferences into the executable set through retraining or constraint injection [10, 11]. 

Second , neuro-symbolic methods expose such an abstraction, but their symbolic edits cannot prop-agate to the controller-level networks; hence, even when the preference is expressible and the plan logically valid, execution may still stay mismatched and physically infeasible [12]. A recent method, Emergent Neural Automaton Policy (ENAP) [13], offers a promising substrate for physically grounded steering. It decomposes a visuomotor policy into a low-level continuous controller and a high-level state-machine skeleton, whose symbolic abstractions can naturally serve as the intermediate representation between the semantic preference and the controller. Because the symbolic abstraction emerges automatically from demonstrations and is thus inherently aligned with the training data distribution, enabling in-domain preference editing. However, extending such representations to support policy steering under preferences remains an open problem. To ensure physically-aware grounding to support efficient steering, we build on ENAP and introduce 

ReStruct , a framework for adapting learned policies to user-specified preferences without retrain-ing. Specifically, to satisfy a user-specified preference, ReStruct first reconfigures the task structure of the base policy while keeping its low-level controller fixed. We use the automaton to represent the preference and incorporate it into the policy’s symbolic skeleton through a synchronous prod-uct. Since modifying the task structure alone can produce physically infeasible behaviors, ReStruct 

subsequently reshapes the action priors provided the skeleton to filter out infeasible behaviors under the new specification, ensuring that the reconfigured structure remains executable by the controller. We validate ReStruct from in-domain preferences to those that are physically in-domain yet seman-tically out-of-domain (OOD) through novel compositions. In summary, our contributions are: • We introduce ReStruct , a steering framework that enables learned policies to follow high-level semantic preferences, ranging from object-centric specifications to temporal logic, offering everyday users a new perspective to reshape their robots through language, without expert-level knowledge or retraining. • The framework provides interpretable and symbolically verifiable steering through its state-machine structure and prototype-based action priors, enabling preference satisfaction to be inspected at both the symbolic and control levels. • The framework improves data efficiency and cross-task adaptation by reusing experience across diverse demonstrations. It enables target-task behavior to be synthesized by injecting preferences into demonstrations from related but distinct tasks. • Extensive experiments show that ReStruct improves preference following without degrad-ing task success and surpasses VLA models by up to 25% on both, indicating strategy adaptation rather than mere preference compliance.

2) Linear Temporal Logic (LTL) Specifications. LTL extends propositional logic with temporal operators that describe how truth values evolve along a trajectory [14]. Given a set of atomic propo-sitions AP , LTL formulas are built from p ∈ AP , Boolean connectives, and the temporal operators 

⃝ (next), ♢ (eventually), □ (always), and U (until). In our settings, these propositions correspond to clusters in the PMM’s observation alphabet Σ, optionally refined by a VLM (e.g., splitting a generic “ pick ” cluster into “ pick red cube ” and “ pick blue cube ”); a preference like “ pick red cube first ” is then expressible as ♢(pick red cube ∧ ⃝ ♢pick blue cube ). We consider a variant of LTL, namely Linear Temporal Logic over Finite Traces ( LTL f ), which is defined over finite-time robot trajectories [15]. Each LTL f formula can be translated into a Deterministic Finite Automaton (DFA) A = ( S, s 0, ΣA, δ A, F ), where ΣA denotes the input alphabet and F ⊆ S is the set of accepting states. s0, δ A denote the start state and transition function, respectively. The DFA accepts exactly the trajectories that satisfy the corresponding formula.

3.2 Action-Prior Bridge: Trajectory Replay 

In the previous discussion, we showed that refining the pD (z|τ ) term of the steered skeleton is vital: the traditional neuro-symbolic method fails in steering because its prior is anchored to the demon-stration that may contain preference-violating behaviors. In our paper, the high-level product (Sec-tion 3.1) yields M2 that admits solely preference-consistent traces, and we need to recompute the action priors for the skeleton to guide physically-aware generation of preference-aligned behavior. Not only for neuro-symbolic methods, most visuomotor policies learn a direct observation-to-action map, compressing the separable structure in the data into an entangled representation. For example (see Fig. 1 (c)), when executing pick(can) → place(shelf) , such a single-mode prior grasps the can from above and incurs a vertical-clearance violation that blocks shelf insertion. ENAP’s per-edge fixed action has the same failure mode. Thus, we redesign the action-prior bridge for ENAP: 

ReStruct stores, for each edge (q, σ ) → q′, the raw list of the action that performs such transitions in the demonstration, to provide diverse priors. Empirically, during skeleton construction, we randomly retain 1/50 ∼ 1/10 of the demonstrations as representative trajectories to make it lightweight. # 5 Limitation 

First , ReStruct builds on ENAP, and extending it to other policy classes remains open, since dis-tilling policies into ENAP incurs a performance gap—future direction is reducing the gap. Second ,both task success and preference compliance depend on the VLM-generated LTL formula. This may be alleviated through iterative, multi-round formula refinement. Third , our steerable scope (Re-mark 3.1) is bounded: scaling to richer OOD regimes and eliciting new skills remain unexplored. 

# 6 Conclusion. 

We propose ReStruct , a zero-shot behavior steering framework that satisfies preferences at inference time by reconfiguring a policy’s task structure without retraining. Specifically, we first transform the preference into an automaton and generate a steered task structure based on it, while keeping the base policy network frozen. An action prior recomputation process is then used to bridge the gap between the modified high-level structure and the original controller. Extensive experiments validate 

ReStruct consistently leads in both success and preference compliance. More broadly, ReStruct 

provides a viable path for everyday users to reshape their robots by language, not retraining. 8A Related Works 

A.1 Preference Specification for Robot Policies 

Deploying a learned policy to real users almost always reveals a gap between what it was trained to do and what each user actually wants. In the domain, preference specification spans a broad spectrum. Scalar comparisons remain canonical: feedback can train rewards [28, 29, 30], and DPO-style updates can align diffusion policies from pairwise preferences without an explicit re-ward [31]. Natural language provides a more flexible substrate, from describing deviations in the source demonstrations [32] to grounding open-vocabulary object goals [32, 33, 34, 35]. Demonstra-tions also encode intent implicitly, from hand-held grippers [36] and web videos [37] to egocentric smart-glass recordings [38] that scale data collection toward zero-shot deployment. Visual goals 

reduce linguistic ambiguity through image-edited subgoals [39] and intentionally sparse sketches [40], while formal specifications encode intent as automata or temporal-logic formulas, increasingly coupled with LLM-based language-to-spec translation [41, 42]. Policy-side preference integration can be grouped into three regimes. Training-time methods re-optimize or fine-tune policies under the preference signal [28, 29, 31, 36, 37, 38]; prompt-level methods condition language-aware VLAs [33] or pass LLM-generated plans to low-level controllers [35]; and neuro-symbolic methods route preferences through symbolic intermediaries layered over the policy, such as LLM-scored affordances over learned skill libraries [43], executable code calling neural primitives [44], or language-translated formal specifications consumed by planners [42]. These approaches expose two structural limitations. Controller-alignment methods offer broad pref-erence expressivity, but require re-optimizing or fine-tuning the policy for each new user preference [28, 31, 36, 37, 38], making them impractical after deployment. Symbolic-routing methods avoid modifying the controller by inserting a symbolic scaffold [35, 41, 42, 43, 44], but this scaffold is typ-ically defined by a fixed designer-specified vocabulary, with a fixed symbol-to-controller interface that prevents symbolic edits from reliably propagating to the executed behavior. What is needed, then, is a steering route that avoids retraining yet still propagates the user’s preference all the way into the controller’s behavior—which is precisely what ReStruct provides. 

A.2 Neuro-symbolic Policies for Manipulation 

Neuro-symbolic methods in manipulation aim to combine the compositional structure and inter-pretability of symbolic reasoning with the perceptual and sensorimotor flexibility of neural net-works. Classical task and motion planning frameworks such as PDDLStream [45] established a clean factoring—discrete symbolic search over manually specified predicates paired with contin-uous motion planners—but their reliance on hand-authored symbolic vocabularies sharply limited adaptability. Subsequent work has therefore pushed each layer of this stack toward learnable sub-stitutes. In the action layer, hierarchical and option-based methods replace fixed primitives with discoverable skills [46], later evolving into great parameterized skills [47, 48] that admit continuous control within otherwise symbolic plans. In the state layer, predicate-learning approaches ground continuous sensorimotor signals into discrete symbols [49, 50]—through neuro-symbolic concept learners [51], or self-supervised automaton extraction directly from visuomotor data [13]. In the model layer, the symbolic transition dynamics themselves are inferred, from probabilistic rules in stochastic domains [52] to graph-neural relational models [53] and hybrid automata for stochastic transitions [54]. A more recent and complementary line offloads the high-level structure to pre-trained foundation models [43, 44, 42]. While these approaches successfully marry learning with symbolic structure, they share a common architectural commitment: the symbolic layer is external to the controller—either hand-authored, learned as a separate module, or generated by a foundation model on top of a frozen low-level policy—and the controller acts as a black box beneath it. ReStruct departs from this pattern by re-computing the policy’s symbolic-level prior under each user preference and propagating it back 9into the low-level controller, so that the preference is realized as a physically-aware steering signal rather than a detached symbolic plan layered above the policy. 

A.3 Inference-time Adaptation of Learned Policies 

A recent line of work asks how to bias a deployed generative policy without retraining it [55, 56]. Most methods operate inside the diffusion denoising loop: human sketches and clicks bias the ac-tion sampling [3], an external dynamics model guides toward visual outcomes [57], or Feynman-Kac particles resample intermediate trajectories under arbitrary rewards [4]. Decoding-level methods in-stead choose among policy proposals—bidirectional decoding searches for actions consistent across timesteps [58] and contrastive decoding refocuses attention on task-relevant objects [59]—without altering policy weights. Latent-space approaches run reinforcement learning over the diffusion pol-icy’s noise input to adapt it online [60]. For VLA-class generalists, pretrained VLMs act as runtime verifiers, selecting action plans whose predicted outcomes align with the VLA’s textual reasoning [61] or with downstream task goals [62]. These methods steer within the action distribution, which restricts the preferences they can handle to those that are approximately differentiable, such as a gradient signal or a sampleable verifier. Genuine steering, however, requires reaching beyond the action distribution: forming a semantic understanding of the real-world complex preference, and feeding that understanding back into the action distribution itself. ReStruct provides this route via computing a symbolic-level interpretation of the user preference and propagating it back through the controller’s action distribution.

C.2 Task Definition 1) StackCube (Fixed) . The agent must stack two colored cubes (Red/Green) in any valid ver-tical configuration. The initial positions of the two cubes are fixed, located on opposite sides of the arm. A trial is considered successful only if the top cube aligns within half-width of the bottom cube, remains static, and is fully released by the gripper. 

2) PegInsert . This task requires picking up a bi-colored peg and inserting either the orange or white end into a randomly positioned hole on the box. The environment features significant variability: peg lengths are sampled from U [0 .15 , 0.20] m and radii from U [0 .015 , 0.025] m, with initial rotation poses randomized. Success is defined as inserting either end at least 0.03 m into the hole (radius rpeg + 0 .02 m). 

3) SeqPushT . A variant with sequential goals of the Push-T task. The robot must maneuver a T-shaped block to two potential target poses in any order. The T-block’s initial pose is fully randomized within a 0.076 m×0.20 m respawn box. Success is achieved if the block reaches the two target poses in sequence, with its Intersection-over-Union (IoU) exceeding 0.90 at each target pose. 

4) Unconstrained . The agent completes five atomic skills— turn on led (LED on), 

turn on bulb (bulb on), rotate block (red block rotated), push left (blue block pushed left), and move slider (slider moved right)—in the order specified by our preference. These skills are mutually independent; success requires all five in the prescribed order. 

5) Causal . The agent completes five atomic skills— rotate block (blue block rotated), 

close drawer (drawer closed), open drawer (drawer opened), push right (pink block pushed right), and push left (blue block pushed left)—in the order specified by our preference. 

close drawer causally depends on open drawer , constraining the order of skill attempts. 

6) CanSorting . It involves sorting three colored cans (red, green, blue) into the same-colored bowls fixed on the table. Our preference, however, asks the agent to instead place each can into a bowl of a target color independent of its own—an OOD behavior demanding compositional gener-alization. 

7) PassTeaPot . The arm grasps a teapot’s handle at a fixed pose and carries it to the table’s far end, with a power strip placed randomly along one side. Demonstrations cover trajectories both near and far from the strip; our preference asks for look-ahead trajectory shaping. 

8) ConeCover . The arm grasps a cone from a fixed source and covers two fixed differently-colored blocks (blue, green) in any order. Cones are visually identical, requiring spatial memory—for ex-ample, lifting the cone already on the blue block and redirecting it onto the green one.

# E Further Ablations: Decoupling Preference from Execution 

Tables 5 and 6 together expose a clean functional decoupling. Reducing the trajethe ctories used to construct the PMM primarily harms preference satisfaction (SRP) while leaving raw task com-pletion (SR) only mildly affected; conversely, undertraining the base policy primarily harms SR while leaving SRP relatively stable, since the symbolic structure that carries the preference handle remains intact. This asymmetry holds across all three Complex Manipulation tasks, including the more dynamics-heavy SeqPushT . The results reveal that preference adherence depends primarily on whether the PMM carries sufficiently rich priors, while overall task performance hinges on the scale of data and training devoted to the base policy.
