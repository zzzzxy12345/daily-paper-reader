# Connect the Dots: Training LLMs for Long-Lifecycle Agents with Cross-Domain Generalization Via Reinforcement Learning 

## Yanxi Chen ∗† , Weijie Shi ∗‡ , Yuexiang Xie †, Boyi Hu §,Yaliang Li †, Bolin Ding †, Jingren Zhou †

Abstract 

This work presents a general framework for training large language models (LLMs) to “Connect the Dots” ( CoD ), a meta-capability required by long-lifecycle agents: as an LLM-based AI agent gets deployed in an environment, it solves a long sequence of tasks while continuously exploring the envi-ronment, learning from its own experiences, and iteratively self-updating its context about the envi-ronment, thereby achieving progressively better performance on future tasks conditioned on the up-dated context. Major components of the CoD framework include: (1) algorithm design and infras-tructure for end-to-end reinforcement learning (RL) with long rollout sequences interleaving solve-task and update-context episodes; (2) tasks and environments for incentivizing and eliciting the targeted meta-capability in LLMs during training, as well as for faithfully measuring progress during evalua-tion. We present proof-of-concept implementations of the CoD framework, including a GRPO-style RL algorithm with fine-grained credit assignment, as well as tasks and environments tailored to the targeted meta-capability (rather than domain-specific LLM capabilities or standard task-by-task RL). Empirical results validate the efficacy of end-to-end RL training in the CoD setting, and demonstrate the potential for out-of-distribution generalization — within the training domains, across different do-mains, and from CoD to Ralph-loop settings — of the elicited meta-capability. Our investigation of CoD connects several lines of prior works, and opens up new opportunities for advancing LLMs and AI agents. To facilitate further research and applications, we release our implementations at 

https://github.com/agentscope-ai/Trinity-RFT/tree/research/cod/examples/research_cod .

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have progressed rapidly in the past few years, evolving from chatbots to reasoning models, and now serving as the backbones for numerous AI agents in real-world applications. We envision that in the near future, further progress in LLMs will bring them to long-lifecycle agentic deployment 

in diverse environments and continual learning at test time .To achieve this vision, an AI agent should be able to “Connect the Dots” ( CoD ). More formally, we define the CoD meta-capability as follows: during long-lifecycle deployment in an environment , an AI agent continuously solves a sequence of different-but-related tasks , while proactively and deliberately exploring the environment and self-updating its context about it, thereby facilitating more effective task solving within the same environment in the future. In contrast to domain-specific capabilities (such as math problem solving and competitive coding), CoD is a meta-capability that can potentially generalize across diverse environments from different domains, enabling the AI agent to self-improve efficiently during deployment and thereby enhancing its task-solving performance [Silver and Sutton, 2025, Rafiee and Sutton, 2026]. Frontier LLMs today still fall short in this regard: with limited CoD meta-capability, an LLM-based agent may easily

We believe that LLMs need to be explicitly trained for the CoD meta-capability, ideally through end-to-end reinforcement learning (RL) in environments with clean reward signals. Indeed, while early LLMs exhibited certain chain-of-thought reasoning capabilities [Wei et al., 2022, Kojima et al., 2022] and agentic intelligence [Yao et al., 2023], reinforcement learning — with properly designed algorithms and environments — has proved effective in driving fundamental improvements in these aspects [OpenAI, 2024, DeepSeek-AI, 2025, Kimi-Team, 2025, Qwen-Team, 2026, Kimi-Team, 2026, GLM-5-Team, 2026]; we anticipate that the same should hold for eliciting the CoD meta-capability in LLMs. On the other hand, standard task-by-task RL essentially trains an LLM to solve each task individually from scratch , which appears misaligned with the CoD meta-capability targeted for long-lifecycle deployment. This motivates us to design and implement a dedicated RL process for CoD ; in this work, we present our initial progress. 

Contributions and organization. We begin by introducing a general CoD framework, visualized in Figure 1 and elaborated in Section 2. CoD-Deploy represents an abstraction of long-lifecycle deployment of an AI agent in an environment, which interleaves episodes for solving new tasks and for self-updating the agent’s context about the environment. CoD-Train denotes the corresponding RL post-training process, whose rollout pattern exactly matches that of CoD-Deploy .One major challenge lies in RL algorithm design, and in particular, credit assignment across solve-task and update-context episodes in a long state-action sequence. We adopt the classical dynamic-programming principle [Bellman, 1957, Sutton et al., 1998]: the objective of each episode is to maximize not only the immediate reward, but also future rewards. Another major challenge is the choice of appropriate tasks and environments [Huang et al., 2025]. For training, we need environments that properly incentivize self-updating an agent’s context about the environment — e.g., revealed clues that were initially hidden, or task-solving skills with validated efficacy — and solving new tasks conditioned on that context rather than from scratch. For evaluation, we need environments that faithfully measure progress in improving the CoD 

meta-capability of LLMs. The CoD framework therefore calls for dedicated task and environment design, as standard RL datasets and LLM benchmarks are not necessarily suitable for these purposes. In an effort to tackle these challenges, we present in Section 3 our concrete implementations of the CoD 

framework, which include RL algorithms and infrastructure, along with environments and tasks tailored to 

CoD . Our empirical results validate the efficacy of end-to-end RL for eliciting the CoD meta-capability. For example, when applying CoD-Train to Qwen3-8B-Instruct on FrozenLake-Obscure environments containing clues that are necessary for successful task solving but initially hidden, the agent’s success rate in solving a task from scratch rises only from 18% to 45% (approximately) due to information-theoretic limits, yet its success rate in solving the fourth task within a sequence — conditioned on its self-updated context 

— increases substantially from 28% to 76%. Moreover, the elicited CoD meta-capability demonstrates potential for in-domain and cross-domain generalization, as well as generalization to Ralph-loop settings [Huntley, 2026], as validated by out-of-distribution evaluation. During our development of CoD , we connect several lines of prior work, including research and practice of “lifelong agents” [Workshop, 2026a], meta reinforcement learning [Duan et al., 2016], inference scaling [Huntley, 2026], among others. A brief review of these lines, along with their connections to and differences from CoD , can be found in Section 4. Finally, while this work provides proof-of-concept implementations of the CoD framework and presents promising empirical results, many research questions remain open and call for further work, e.g., refining the design of RL algorithms and environments dedicated to CoD , continuing to scale up CoD along various dimensions, and integrating CoD into existing LLM post-training pipelines; detailed discussions can be found in Section 5.

Figure 1: A visualization of CoD-Deploy and CoD-Train (compared with standard task-by-task RL), where A and B denote different environments for training, and M denotes a new environment for deployment or evaluation. Each block represents one rollout episode for solving a task xi (which could be in itself a long-horizon multi-turn task) or updating the agent’s context zi about the current environment. 

• CoD-Deploy is an abstraction of long-lifecycle agentic deployment in an environment M , which interleaves episodes for solving a task xMi and for updating the agent’s context from zMi to zMi+1 . This process can be understood as (gradient-free) online learning via trial-and-errors. 

• CoD-Train represents the rollout mechanism in RL post-training dedicated to CoD , and the corre-sponding RL algorithm for training model weights. Note that the rollout pattern in CoD-Train is simply the same as in CoD-Deploy , covering diverse environments A, B, . . . for generalization. From the perspective of hierarchical reinforcement learning , CoD-Train goes up by one level in the hierarchy, compared to standard task-by-task RL. Indeed, the definition of one rollout trajectory in LLM-RL has evolved from a sequence of tokens for a single-turn task (e.g., RLHF [Ouyang et al., 2022, Bai et al., 2022] and reasoning-oriented RLVR [OpenAI, 2024, DeepSeek-AI, 2025]), to a sequence of turns for a long-horizon agentic task [Zhou et al., 2024]; CoD-Train takes it one step further, to a sequence of tasks for long-lifecycle agentic deployment in an environment. 

End-to-end reinforcement learning. Given the problem formulation in CoD-Train , we aim for effec-tive end-to-end RL training. One crucial aspect here is credit assignment for a long state-action sequence consisting of multiple solve-task and update-context episodes, each of which might already contain multiple turns of agent-environment interaction and thus a large number of tokens. Fortunately, we have one critical observation: suppose that there is (at least) an outcome reward for solving each task, then the total number of reward values in the end-to-end state-action sequence will grow proportionally with the number of tasks, i.e., the density of reward signals is independent of the total number of tasks. We follow the fundamental dynamic-programming principle [Bellman, 1957, Sutton et al., 1998] for credit assignment in CoD-Train : each episode should aim for maximizing not only the immediate reward, but also future rewards. In an update-context episode, the agent performs deliberate condensation of its previous experiences and incorporate it into the updated context, which should contribute to better performance in solving future tasks within the same environment. In a solve-task episode, the agent takes action — 3conditioned on its self-updated context — while balancing exploitation and exploration, i.e., trying to solve the current task while simultaneously exploring the environment and collecting new information / discovering task-solving skills that are worthy of being incorporated into the updated context. 

Tasks and environments for training and evaluation. Choosing the right tasks and environments for CoD decides whether CoD-Train can effectively elicit the targeted meta-capability as we desire, and whether evaluation results in CoD-Deploy align well with the LLM’s performance in real-world long-lifecycle scenarios and faithfully reflect the progress of LLM development. 

• Ideal tasks and environments for training should ensure that an agent has sufficient incentives for iteratively updating its context about the environment and transferring it across different tasks. This is true, for example, if successful task solving requires certain information about the environment that the agent cannot possibly access prior to deployment; in this case, solving each task independently would hit an information-theoretic performance limit, and the agent is thus incentivized to continuously accumulate knowledge about the environment and transfer it across tasks. In other scenarios, the agent might be incentivized to propose and try out different task-solving skills, observe environmental feedback, and then focus on skills with validated efficacy for future tasks within the same environment. 

• Similarly, ideal tasks and environments for evaluation need to align well with long-lifecycle agentic deployment in challenging real-world scenarios, where the best overall performance can be achieved only if the agent proactively maintains its context about the environment and solves new tasks conditioned on it (rather than from scratch). Nonetheless, more generic tasks and environments might still be worthy of consideration: 

• For training, if it is possible in theory to achieve maximum reward for each task by solving it from scratch (without any extra context), then there is a risk that CoD-Train might degenerate into standard task-by-task RL. Yet another possibility is that CoD-Train might exhibit different learning dynamics than those of standard RL (e.g., due to the natural integration of text-based environment feedback into the rollout process), which could still lead to acquisition of the CoD meta-capability, better out-of-distribution generalization, and/or higher sample efficiency than standard RL. 

• For evaluation, any existing benchmark can be converted to a CoD counterpart, by regarding the whole benchmark (or a certain subset of it) as a collection of tasks from the same environment. Applying 

CoD-Deploy in this setting could lead to better context — generated automatically by the agent itself with a handful of trial-and-errors — that improves the agent’s performance on the same benchmark. The CoD meta-capability also plays a role in a Ralph-loop setting [Huntley, 2026] where an agent attempts repeatedly to solve the same task, or within a single episode for solving a long-horizon task that requires delicate context management. 

Primary research questions. As we set out on implementing the CoD framework, several research questions of interest naturally arise, for example: 

• What is the suitable RL algorithm for CoD-Train , e.g., how to calculate advantages and gradient updates? Is it possible to achieve more effective and efficient training by taking advantage of the particular rollout pattern in CoD-Train ? Conversely, is it possible to design a better rollout pattern that allows more effective RL training? 

• What are some concrete examples of appropriate tasks and environments for training and evaluating the CoD meta-capability in LLMs? To what extent will the learned CoD meta-capability generalize? # 3 Proof-of-concept implementations and experiments 

In this section, we present our implementations of the CoD framework, as well as preliminary empirical results that validate the efficacy of end-to-end RL in eliciting the CoD meta-capability. 

## 3.1 RL algorithm and infrastructure 

In terms of reward design, we consider tasks with outcome rewards for simplicity in our current implemen-tations. We thus assign a task-wise outcome reward to each solve-task episode within a rollout trajectory in CoD-Train . For each update-context episode, we assign only a small format reward, since there is no other direct reward signal for it. We adopt a GRPO-style [Shao et al., 2024] training algorithm in CoD-Train , which requires no critic model and assumes access to multiple rollout trajectories for the same sequence of tasks. Note that GRPO originally assumes a single outcome reward for each rollout trajectory, whereas in CoD-Train , each rollout trajectory contains a sequence of reward values for multiple episodes. To account for this, we define the return of each episode as the mean reward of the current episode plus future solve-task episodes. Then, we regard episodes at the same position in its corresponding rollout trajectory as one group, using their averaged return as the baseline for advantage calculation. Figure 2 presents a visualization of credit assignment and advantage calculation in our RL algorithm tailored to CoD-Train . To resolve training instability observed in our early experiments, we further add a heuristic augmentation that balances positive and negative gradients by re-weighting training samples adaptively. See Appendix A for full details of our final RL algorithm. On the infrastructure side, we build on Trinity-RFT [Pan et al., 2025], an LLM-RL framework with a modular and decoupled design that facilitates convenient customization of various components involved in the RL pipeline. We implement an environment-wise meta-workflow in the form of CoD-Deploy , so that any task and its corresponding task-wise rollout workflow can be readily plugged in after minor modifications. For simplicity, our current implementations focus on a minimalistic instantiation of cross-episode context, also termed as “ hint ”; it is essentially a single piece of text that will be appended to the system prompt in a solve-task episode. We find it sufficient for proof-of-concept implementations of the CoD framework; one important direction for future work is to generalize the update-context mechanism, e.g., involving persistent 5memory banks or Markdown files for Agent Skills [Anthropic, 2025a], which could be crucial for performance improvements in more challenging scenarios. 

## 3.2 Tasks and environments 

As explained in Section 2, generic tasks and environments for standard task-by-task RL are not necessarily appropriate for training and evaluation of the CoD meta-capability. We thus design and implement dedicated environments for our investigation of CoD , which are elaborated in the following. 

FrozenLake-Obscure is analogous to the classic FrozenLake game [Towers et al., 2026], but with obscurity in the environments. In a standard FrozenLake task, an agent navigates on a 2D grid map by choosing one action from moving up/down/left/right at each step, and needs to reach the goal position within a limited number of steps while avoiding holes in the map. The key difference in FrozenLake-Obscure is that the action space now becomes A/B/C/D, and for each new environment, the mapping between A/B/C/D and up/down/left/right is randomly permuted and unknown a priori . This imposes an information-theoretic limit on the success rate that an agent can possibly achieve when solving each task individually without additional context 1. The agent, when deployed in a new environment, is thus 

incentivized to maintain its context and transfer it across episodes when solving a sequence of tasks within this environment. A good “hint” for a FrozenLake-Obscure environment should thus contain the ground-truth mapping between A/B/C/D and up/down/left/right, derived by the agent itself from previous solve-task episodes. Despite its simplicity, FrozenLake-Obscure contains the fundamental elements of long-lifecycle agentic deployment, making it a minimal sanity-check testbed for CoD , e.g., for validating a RL algorithm dedicated to CoD-Train , or evaluating generalization of an LLM’s CoD meta-capability. 

Alchemy-Random is another game scenario, designed by the same principles and inspired by the classic 

Alchemy benchmark [Wang et al., 2021]. Each Alchemy-Random environment has its own set of elements and recipes (i.e., which pair of elements can be combined to synthesize another element); these are randomly initialized and then fixed for each environment. In each task, an agent is asked to synthesize a targeted element within a limited number of steps, without any prior knowledge about the ground-truth recipes initially when deployed in a new environment. A good hint in the Alchemy-Random domain should ideally contain valid recipes and failed combinations — discovered by the agent itself in previous solve-task episodes — which facilitate more efficient searching when solving a new task; it might also contain task-solving skills tailored to this domain, e.g., advanced strategies for systematically deciding which combination to try out at each step. Compared with FrozenLake-Obscure , Alchemy-Random allows more diverse environments, a wider range of configurable difficulty for the tasks, and richer behaviors by the agent. 

TerminalSimulator is a simulation scenario that tests an agent’s capabilities in daily tasks relevant to Linux / MacOS / Windows terminals, such as file manipulation and transferring. The agent is asked to solve each task within a limited number of steps; for each step, the agent observes the current environment state, executes a terminal command, and receives feedback from the environment. Since the optimal approach for solving similar tasks can vary depending on characteristics of the environments (e.g., operating systems, or sizes of the files to be manipulated), it is generally useful for the agent to explore the current environment and unveil its characteristics while solving a specific task.

# 4 Related works 

Research and practice of “lifelong agents”. There has been a surge of research on lifelong agents, which includes a dedicated workshop at ICLR 2026 [Workshop, 2026a]. On the theoretical side, recent work has argued that context management is crucial for boosting the computational expressivity of an LLM-based agent system [Cui et al., 2026]. On the practical side, there is growing interest in LLM-based AI agents with long-lifecycle deployment, in which an agent updates its own context — e.g., in the form of persistent memory banks or skill sets — and thereby self-improves its task-solving performance over time. Representative examples include agent frameworks such as Hermes-Agent and related systems [NousResearch, 2026, OpenClaw, 2025, Anthropic, 2025b, AgentScope-AI, 2026, Chhikara et al., 2025]. A notable gap in the literature is the lack of dedicated LLM post-training for the meta-capability required by a lifelong agent. As a result, practical AI systems often depend on human-crafted agent scaffolds with sophisticated hand-designed components. A related limitation is that frontier LLMs still fall short in envi-ronments with underspecification [Pu et al., 2026], where agents must adapt online to a new environment, balance exploration and exploitation, and take actions conditioned on the context [Dou et al., 2026]. In contrast to these requirements, agents powered by current LLMs often act based on incorrect priors with over-confidence, or lose coherence after multiple rounds of interaction with the environment [Laban et al., 2026, Wei et al., 2025]. An important design question in this area is what should be updated during learning: the context, the model weights, or both. Some approaches optimize context alone, such as prompts, few-shot examples, or skills [Opsahl-Ong et al., 2024, Wang et al., 2024, Agrawal et al., 2026, Ouyang et al., 2026], while others combine context optimization with updating model weights [Soylu et al., 2024]. More recent work [Tiwari et al., 2026] integrates both perspectives, but trains the policy model for solving tasks via RL while relying on an external, fixed LLM to update the agent’s states or context. Another recent work [Yi et al., 2026] instead fixes the solve-task LLM and trains the context-management LLM via end-to-end RL for long-horizon tasks. 

CoD offers an alternative way to integrate both components seamlessly: it trains model weights during 

CoD-Train so that the LLM becomes proficient not only at solving tasks, but also at self-updating its own context during CoD-Deploy .

9Meta reinforcement learning. CoD is closely related to meta reinforcement learning [Botvinick et al., 2019, Stadie et al., 2019, Beck et al., 2025, Han et al., 2026], especially the RL 2 paradigm [Duan et al., 2016]. In the original RL 2 formulation, multiple solve-task episodes are concatenated into a single rollout trajectory, and the hidden state of a recurrent neural network (RNN) serves as the context transferred across episodes. One important difference is that, with an RNN, context is produced using a fixed amount of compute and has a fixed size; in contrast, with an LLM, context can have adaptive size and can be generated through a thinking process with adaptive compute, making it more computationally expressive [Merrill and Sabharwal, 2024, Li et al., 2024, Cui et al., 2026]. A second difference is that LLMs introduce new opportunities for out-of-distribution generalization, compared to RL in the pre-LLM era. Several recent works attempt to adapt RL 2 to LLMs, including LaMer [Jiang et al., 2026], MAGE [Yang et al., 2026], and Orbit [Lin et al., 2026]. These works differ from the present work in their problem settings and RL algorithms. In all of these works, the agent performs a sequence of episodes or attempts for the same task. This setting is closer to sequential test-time scaling (discussed later in this section) or a Ralph loop [Huntley, 2026], rather than to the classic form of meta RL. In such settings, the sequence of episodes terminates upon success, whereas in CoD the agent must continue solving newly imposed tasks throughout deployment. LaMer [Jiang et al., 2026] and MAGE [Yang et al., 2026] employ the GiGPO algorithm [Feng et al., 2026], which assumes that repeated environment states, called “anchor states,” can be identified across task-solving trajectories. CoD does not make this assumption. Orbit [Lin et al., 2026] uses GRPO and treats the sum of rewards over a sequence of episodes as the final outcome reward. Such coarse-grained credit assignment inevitably limits the sequence lengths for which effective training is possible. In our early experiments, this approach was not feasible for effective training on FrozenLake-Obscure , whereas our fine-grained credit assignment enables effective training across a broad range of settings. 

LLM inference scaling. CoD-Deploy is also related to LLM inference scaling at test time. In this setting, an agent attempts to recursively improve its performance [Workshop, 2026b] through repeated LLM calls, which may be organized sequentially, in parallel, or through a combination of both [Lightman et al., 2024, Snell et al., 2025, Chen et al., 2026]. Practical examples include the Ralph loop [Huntley, 2026] and auto-research systems [Novikov et al., 2025]. The connection is straightforward: these settings can be viewed as a special case of CoD-Deploy in which the task sequence x0, x 1, x 2, . . . happens to consist of repeated instances of exactly the same task. Recent works have also proposed RL training methods specifically aimed at improving the capability for test-time inference scaling [Hu et al., 2026, Kimi-Team, 2026]. Inference scaling can also be incorporated into the rollout stage of RL. Examples include reflection-and-retry methods [Shi et al., 2026, Song et al., 2026] and approaches that incorporate skills during rollout [Lu et al., 2026]. The objective of inference scaling in these methods is to generate rollout trajectories of higher quality. However, the policy model is still trained to solve tasks from scratch, as the additional context (e.g., reflection or skills) is removed when rollout trajectories are converted into training samples for gradient computation. By contrast, CoD explicitly trains the LLM to adapt to a new environment and solve new tasks conditioned on its maintained and continuously updated context. 

# 5 Discussions 

Limitations and future work. This work has introduced a general CoD framework, provided proof-of-concept implementations of it, and presented promising empirical results. Still, many research questions remain open and call for further works. For example, there remain some heuristic augmentations and potential caveats in our current GRPO-style RL algorithm (cf. Section 3.1 and Appendix A); future works might aim for a more principled and theoretically grounded RL algorithm with better empirical performance. Moreover, future works might continuously scale up CoD along various dimensions, for example: 

• Designing more diverse and challenging environments (beyond FrozenLake-Obscure and Alchemy-Random ) for training and evaluation of the CoD meta-capability; 

• Validating CoD in broader settings, e.g., efficacy with longer task sequences in potentially non-stationary environments, or OOD generalization across a wider range of environments; 10 • Extending the CoD framework and implementations to more powerful context-management mecha-nisms (beyond rewriting a “hint”), or more flexible rollout patterns than those visualized in Figure 1. 

Integration into LLM post-training pipelines? It is worth noting that CoD is complementary (rather than alternative) to the standard task-by-task RL paradigm that aims for enhancing domain-specific capa-bilities, akin to the relation between fluid and crystallized intelligence [Chollet, 2019]. A natural question is thus about how to integrate CoD into existing LLM post-training pipelines. One straightforward idea is to regard CoD-Train as an extra sequential stage. Another potential idea is to first train a CoD teacher model by CoD-Train , and then use it for model merging — together with other domain-specific teacher models — via on-policy distillation [Lu and Lab, 2025, Qwen-Team, 2025, 2026, DeepSeek-AI, 2026, GLM-5-Team, 2026]. We are excited to see what CoD can potentially bring to the next generation of frontier LLMs.

Remarks. Although our final RL algorithm works reasonably well in our experiments, there is still room for improvements. (1) We use the average of rewards-to-go as the return for each episode, which might become problematic as the length of task sequences scales up. In this case, it could be beneficial to incorporate discounting or a finite sliding window for calculating returns. (2) Another potential caveat in our baseline calculation is that, episodes at the same position from multiple rollout trajectories are not fully comparable. In particular, episodes from different rollout trajectories for solving the same task xi start with different context zi; update-context episodes at the same position also face a similar issue. While choices of the 17 baseline do not affect the expectation of the policy gradient estimate (according to classic RL theory), they do make differences in practical finite-sample settings. Future work might aim for a more principled and theoretically grounded RL algorithm tailored to CoD-Train .
