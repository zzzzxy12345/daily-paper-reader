## PathRouter: Aligning Rewards with Retrieval Quality in Agentic Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation 

Bo Wang 1, Heyan Huang 1, Yaolin Li 1, Wei Tang 2, Yuan Zhang 2, Wenbo Li 2,Mingze Gao 1, Ge Shi 1,Chong Feng 11Beijing Institute of Technology 

2 Joy Future Academy 

Abstract 

Agentic GraphRAG trains language-model agents to iteratively retrieve and reason over graph-structured evidence, enabling more ac-curate and context-aware decision-making by efficiently navigating complex information net-works. However, outcome-only reinforce-ment learning suffers from answer-path re-ward aliasing , where correct answers may come from shortcuts rather than useful evi-dence paths. It also exhibits search-update am-biguity , as scalar trajectory-level feedback does not indicate which retrieval actions to adjust. To mitigate these shortcomings, we present PATH ROUTER , a path-aware training frame-work for agentic GraphRAG. P ATH ROUTER 

jointly evaluates each trajectory along answer correctness and evidence-path overlap, yielding four trajectory categories with differentiated GRPO advantage scaling that suppresses short-cut reinforcement while preserving evidence-seeking behavior. For evidence-poor trajecto-ries, a frozen gold-evidence teacher provides token-level KL guidance on reasoning and search-query tokens, excluding answer tokens to avoid direct response imitation. Experiments on six QA benchmarks across three model sizes show that P ATH ROUTER consistently improves answer F1 and evidence-path overlap, achiev-ing average F1 gains of 3.1 on 3B and 4.9 on 7B models compared to a strong baseline.

ing and search-update ambiguity. To resolve re-ward aliasing, P ATH ROUTER evaluates each tra-jectory along two axes: answer correctness and evidence-path overlap (EO). By categorizing tra-jectories into four types based on these axes, the framework applies differentiated GRPO advantage scaling to suppress reinforcement of shortcut trajec-tories while preserving evidence-seeking behavior, ensuring that policy updates reflect retrieval quality rather than only final answer correctness. To mitigate search-update ambiguity, PATH ROUTER selectively invokes a frozen gold-evidence teacher for evidence-poor trajecto-ries. The teacher, prompted with gold supporting passages, provides token-level KL supervision on reasoning and search-query tokens, while excluding answer tokens to avoid direct response imitation. This guidance supplies actionable information for refining search strategies at the token level, allowing the agent to identify which entities, relations, or queries need adjustment to uncover missing evidence paths. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We identify answer-path reward aliasing as a failure mode for agentic GraphRAG and pro-pose route-conditioned GRPO advantage scaling based on jointly evaluating answer correctness and evidence-path overlap. • We introduce a selective gold-evidence teacher that applies token-level KL to reasoning and search-query tokens for evidence-poor trajecto-ries, resolving search-update ambiguity without directly imitating final answers. • We demonstrate consistent improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods across six QA benchmarks and three model sizes, with strong cross-dataset OOD transfer suggesting that path-aware training yields generalizable retrieval be-havior rather than dataset-specific shortcuts. 

2 Related Work 

RAG and GraphRAG. Retrieval-augmented gen-eration (RAG; Lewis et al., 2020) grounds LLMs in external evidence, but chunk-based retrieval lacks explicit relational modeling (Gao et al., 2024). GraphRAG methods address this by organizing knowledge as entity-relation graphs (Edge et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2023), with path-based strategies exposing multi-hop reason-ing chains via relational pruning (Chen et al., 2025) or iterative traversal (Liu et al., 2025). However, these methods primarily optimize graph construc-tion, path selection, or traversal heuristics; they do not directly train the generator to align its reason-ing with annotated evidence paths. P ATH ROUTER 

treats evidence-path overlap as a first-class train-ing signal, routing updates based on whether the agent’s retrieval covers the gold reasoning chain. 

Reinforcement Learning for Retrieval Agents. 

RL has become central to LLM reasoning, with GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) providing scalable group-relative optimization popularized by DeepSeek-R1 (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025). Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) and R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025) apply GRPO to chunk-based retrieval-augmented reasoning, while Graph-R1 (Luo et al., 2025a) ex-tends this to graph-structured knowledge. These systems primarily rely on outcome-level answer rewards, which can conflate retrieval quality with parametric knowledge and reinforce spurious short-cuts. P ATH ROUTER introduces evidence-path over-lap as a second diagnostic axis, enabling a 2 ×2 tra-jectory taxonomy that distinguishes faithful from lucky success and applies route-conditioned advan-tage scaling accordingly. 

Knowledge Distillation and Sample Routing. 

On-policy distillation (Ross et al., 2011) comple-ments RL by providing token-level supervision on the student’s own rollouts. Privileged-context teachers condition on gold evidence (Ye et al., 2026; Zhao et al., 2026) or unify distillation with GRPO (Xu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2026). Sam-ple routing directs trajectories to different sig-nals: SRPO (Li et al., 2026) routes by correctness, GiGPO (Feng et al., 2025) decomposes advantages hierarchically, and RLSD (Yang et al., 2026) high-lights answer-token leakage risks. These methods generally apply teacher signals uniformly or route by answer correctness alone. P ATH ROUTER condi-tions distillation on evidence-path overlap: a frozen teacher provides token-level KL only for evidence-poor trajectories, restricted to reasoning and query tokens, reducing answer leakage while targeting search-update ambiguity. 3 Approach 

3.1 Task Formulation 

We formalize agentic GraphRAG as a multi-turn agent-environment interaction following Luo et al. (2025a). Given a knowledge graph G constructed from a document collection, an LLM-based agent 

πθ interacts with a graph retrieval environment to answer a query q. At each turn t, the agent ei-ther emits a reasoning segment rt within <think> 

tags followed by a search query qt within <query> 

tags, or emits a final answer a within <answer> 

tags to terminate the trajectory. The environ-ment processes qt and returns retrieved passages within <knowledge> tags as the environment ob-servation ot. A reasoning trajectory is τ =(( r1, q 1, o 1), . . . , (rT , q T , o T ), a ), where T is the number of retrieval turns. Building on this formulation, P ATH ROUTER ex-tends GRPO with a path-aware design. Trajecto-ries are evaluated along answer correctness and evidence-path overlap (EO) to modulate GRPO ad-vantage scaling, distinguishing shortcut answers from evidence-seeking ones. For evidence-poor trajectories, a selective gold-evidence teacher pro-vides token-level KL guidance on reasoning and search-query steps without imitating final answers, encouraging retrieval that is both answer-accurate and evidence-faithful.

The weights serve distinct purposes: • C ↑ P ↑: Full weight (wfull ). The answer is correct and evidence overlap is high. • C ↑ P ↓: Reduced weight (wdown ). Answer is correct but evidence overlap is low, indicating a potential shortcut. Reduced weight mitigates over-reinforcement of trajectories that achieve the correct answer without proper evidence re-trieval. • C ↓ P ↑: Attenuated weight (wpreserve ). Evidence overlap is high but the answer is wrong, which is likely due to a final-reasoning failure. Atten-uated weight mitigates over-penalizing useful retrieval behavior. • C ↓ P ↓: Full weight (wfull ). Both answer cor-rectness and evidence overlap are low. 

3.3 Distillation for Retrieval-Token 

Route-conditioned scaling adjusts the magnitude of policy updates but leaves each trajectory with a single scalar advantage that does not specify which search actions should change. For evidence-poor trajectories ( Pi < θ P ) identified by the routing di-agnostics in §3.2, we address this search-update ambiguity by invoking a gold-evidence teacher that provides token-level distributional supervision on reasoning and search-query tokens, converting trajectory-level evidence failure into localized guid-ance for retrieval improvement. The teacher is used 

only during training , whereas at inference the stu-dent acts without any privileged information. Inspired by Ye et al. (2026), we use the frozen reference model as the teacher. Crucially, the teacher does not generate its own trajectory during training, but instead scores the student’s on-policy rollout under a privileged context. For each train-ing sample (q, G, S gt , a ∗), the student first samples a trajectory τi. At each token position t, both the student and teacher condition on the same response prefix r<t from this trajectory, differing only in their respective prompt context: STUDENT: πθ(· | q, G, r <t ), (5) TEACHER: πref (· | q, G, S gt , r <t ), (6) where Sgt is the set of gold supporting passages appended to the teacher’s prompt. The teacher thus provides evidence-informed next-token guidance on the student’s own on-policy trajectory, rather than sequence-level imitation of a separate teacher rollout. The frozen reference model ensures a sta-ble distributional target throughout training. 4 Experiments 

We evaluate P ATH ROUTER to answer the following research questions: RQ1 : Does P ATH ROUTER im-prove answer accuracy and evidence quality over existing baselines? (§4.2) RQ2 : Which compo-nents drive path faithfulness, and how does rout-ing change trajectory-level behavior? (§4.3, §4.4) 

RQ3 : How does teacher KL interact with model ca-pacity, and do the learned retrieval strategies trans-fer across datasets? (§4.5, §4.6) 

4.1 Experimental Setup Datasets. We follow the evaluation protocol of Graph-R1 (Luo et al., 2025a) and use six QA bench-marks: three multi-hop datasets ( HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), 2WikiMultiHopQA (Ho et al., 2020), and MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022)) that pro-vide gold supporting facts enabling path faith-fulness evaluation, and three single-hop datasets (NQ (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), PopQA (Mallen et al., 2023), and TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017)) for broader coverage. 

Baselines. Following Graph-R1 (Luo et al., 2025a), we compare the following methods: 

GPT-4o-mini-based methods as references: 

GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2025), LightRAG (Guo et al., 2025), PathRAG (Chen et al., 2025), Hip-poRAG2 (Gutiérrez et al., 2025), and Hyper-GraphRAG (Luo et al., 2025b). 

Non-retrieval approaches: NaiveGeneration 

(direct LLM), SFT (Zheng et al., 2024) (supervised fine-tuning), and R1 (Shao et al., 2024) (GRPO without retrieval). 

Chunk-based retrieval: StandardRAG (Lewis et al., 2020), Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025), and R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025). 

Graph-based agentic retrieval: Graph-R1 (Luo et al., 2025a) (our primary baseline). 

Evaluation Metrics. We report token-level F1 ,

Exact Match (EM) , and GPT-4o-mini evaluation (G-E) for answer quality, along with Supporting Fact F1 (SF-F1) and Unsupported Answer Rate (UAR, ↓) for path faithfulness. Formal definitions are in Appendix A.

4.3 Ablation Study 

Table 2 assesses how the reward design, teacher KL objective, and routing mechanism each contribute to path faithfulness. In the reward group, removing the path reward rp primarily lowers SF-F1 while retaining much of the answer F1, indicating that this signal improves evidence quality rather than merely supporting answer prediction. By contrast, removing either the exploration bonus re or the lazy penalty rl substantially reduces retrieval turns to approximately one and causes broad degradation across all metrics, showing that both terms are es-sential for sustaining multi-step evidence seeking. The timeout ro and redundancy rd penalties yield more modest but consistent regularization benefits. In the teacher KL group, removing KL pre-serves multi-turn behavior but reduces answer F1, whereas applying KL uniformly underperforms se-lective KL, suggesting that teacher guidance is most effective when focused on evidence-poor tra-jectories. In the routing group, removing route scaling or reducing the 2D taxonomy to a single axis degrades both F1 and SF-F1, demonstrating that answer correctness and evidence-path overlap provide complementary supervision signals. This analysis examines whether route-conditioned training promotes evidence-path exploration be-yond improvements in answer F1. The overall shift from the P ↓ column to the P ↑ column demon-strates that P ATH ROUTER improves evidence ac-quisition independently of answer correctness, sup-porting the conclusion that route-conditioned train-ing encourages genuine evidence-path exploration rather than exploiting parametric answer priors.

4.6 Cross-Dataset Transfer 

To assess whether path-faithful retrieval behav-ior generalizes beyond individual training distri-butions, we train P ATH ROUTER on each dataset separately and evaluate on all others. Figure 4 reports the OOD generalization ratio for each train– eval pair. P ATH ROUTER achieves a highly uniform transfer profile, with all off-diagonal ratios exceed-ing 89% and an average ratio of 95.7%, suggesting that path-aware routing and selective query guid-ance encourage retrieval strategies that are not tied to the distributional characteristics of any single training set. In comparison, Search-R1 and Graph-R1 achieve average ratios of 70.6% and 85.8% re-spectively (Appendix D.3). 

5 Conclusion 

We introduced PATH ROUTER , a path-aware training framework for agentic GraphRAG that addresses the credit assignment limita-tions of outcome-only reinforcement learning. PATH ROUTER evaluates trajectories by answer cor-rectness and evidence-path overlap, then modulates GRPO advantage weights to suppress shortcut rein-forcement while preserving faithful-but-wrong ex-plorations. For evidence-poor trajectories, a frozen gold-evidence teacher provides selective top-K

token-level KL guidance on reasoning and search-query tokens, with masking and warmup to prevent answer imitation and stabilize training. This path-conditioned design enables more targeted policy optimization than uniform distillation or outcome-only reinforcement. Limitations 

Our approach introduces additional hyperparame-ters for routing and teacher scheduling, which may require tuning when applied to smaller models or new domains. In addition, the path-aware reward encourages more exploration turns during training, and the teacher forward passes for selective KL add per-step cost. P ATH ROUTER thus trades training efficiency for improved faithfulness and robustness.

The datasets and corpora used in our experiments are all open-access resources, and no private or sensitive information is involved. The models and APIs adopted in this work are obtained from pub-licly accessible platforms and are used in accor-dance with their respective terms and policies. Our study focuses on improving retrieval and reason-ing over public knowledge corpora, and we do not anticipate any direct ethical concerns arising from this research.

We define the five evaluation metrics used through-out the paper. 

Token-level F1. Given a predicted answer a

and gold answer a∗, both tokenized into word sets, precision P = |a ∩ a∗|/|a| and recall R = |a ∩

a∗|/|a∗|, then F1 = 2 P R/ (P + R). This is the standard SQuAD-style token-level F1 (Rajpurkar et al., 2016). 

Exact Match (EM). EM equals 1 if the normal-ized predicted answer string exactly matches the normalized gold answer, and 0 otherwise. Nor-malization includes lowercasing, removing articles, punctuation, and extra whitespace.
