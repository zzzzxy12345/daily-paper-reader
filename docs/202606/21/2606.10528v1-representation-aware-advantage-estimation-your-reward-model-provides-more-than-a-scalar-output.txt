Abstract 

Current reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) methods primarily rely on scalar rewards from a trained reward model (RM). While effective, scalar rewards are often noisy and fail to capture fine-grained prefer-ence differences, whereas RM hidden states encode richer semantic and preference infor-mation. We introduce the representation-aware advantage estimation, which leverages RM hid-den states and models them as auxiliary sig-nals for better advantage estimation. Specif-ically, we propose the Graph-based Advan-tage Estimation (GraphAE), treat each sam-pled group as a graph, where nodes corre-spond to responses and edges capture their similarity in the RM hidden space. Then ad-vantages are computed via graph propagation, enabling each sample to incorporate contextual information from its neighbors. GraphAE is lightweight and can be seamlessly integrated into existing group-based RL algorithms. We apply GraphAE to GRPO, GSPO and RLOO, and conduct extensive experiments on differ-ent models and benchmarks. Empirical results show consistent improvements across three benchmarks, with gains of up to + 6.3 on Arena-Hard-v0.1, + 8.27 on AlpacaEval 2.0, and + 0.22 on MT-Bench. These results demonstrate that leveraging RM representations leads to more sample efficient and robust RLHF 1.

As a result, the advantage estimates computed in many online RL algorithms based on such reward signals can be inaccurate or noisy (Zhang et al., 2025). To address this limitation, we aim to identify a more expressive signal that can better capture fine-grained differences among responses. Recent stud-ies on interpretable RMs suggest that intermediate representations encode rich, fine-grained informa-tion related to preference and reward signals that is not fully reflected in the final scalar output (Wang et al., 2024c; Zhang et al., 2026). We therefore utilize the RM’s hidden state representations prior to the final scalar projection for advantage estima-tion. Building on these representations, we move beyond scalar advantage estimation and introduce a Representation-Aware Advantage Estimation. In specific, we propose the Graph-based Advan-tage Estimation (GraphAE), a structured advantage estimator built upon RM representations. Given a set of sampled responses, we construct a similarity graph based on their RM representations, where edge weights reflect pairwise similarity. We then estimate advantages by solving a graph-regularized objective that balances fidelity to the original scalar rewards with smoothness over the graph (Smola and Kondor, 2003; Zhu et al., 2003). This yields a closed-form solution that can be efficiently solved in practice. The resulting rewards integrate both individual reward signals and local structural infor-mation, leading to a more informative and robust estimator. It is lightweight and can be seamlessly integrated into existing group-based RL algorithms by replacing the original advantage estimator, such as GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), GSPO (Zheng et al., 2025), and RLOO (Ahmadian et al., 2024). GraphAE consistently improves performance over strong baselines on dif-ferent RL algorithms and backbones, with gains of up to +6.3 on Arena-Hard, +8.27 on AlpacaE-val, and +0.22 on MT-Bench, demonstrating im-proved alignment quality and robustness. More-over, GraphAE reduces within-group reward vari-ance and accelerates convergence. GraphAE is robust across hyperparameter choices and adds minimal computation during advantage estimation, showing that leveraging RM representations leads to more stable and efficient RLHF. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Reinforcement Learning for LLMs 

Reinforcement learning has emerged as a prevalent method for fine-tuning LLMs, with PPO (Schul-man et al., 2017) and its variations (Ramamurthy et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023) being the most preva-lent methods. These techniques largely adhere to the actor-critic paradigm (Sutton et al., 1998), which significantly increases training complexity and computational resource consumption. Many re-cent studies have sought to mitigate this limitation. Methods such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) and its variants (Meng et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024) bypass reward modeling and the actor-critic learning framework by directly learning from preferences. However, existing re-search indicates that due to their offline nature, these approaches exhibit a performance gap com-pared to online RL (Xu et al., 2024). In contrast to actor-critic and offline methods, recent work ex-plores group-based policy optimization (Shao et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025b), which removes the need for a value function by leverag-ing multiple responses per prompt. For example, GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) computes advantages via group-relative rewards, avoiding critic training while remaining efficient. GSPO (Zheng et al., 2025) improves stability by moving from token- to sequence-level optimization, and RLOO (Ahma-dian et al., 2024) uses a leave-one-out baseline for variance reduction. Despite these differences, they all rely on scalar rewards and treat samples inde-pendently, ignoring their structural relationships. 

2.2 Reward Models 

Reward models (RMs) are central to RLHF, serv-ing as learned proxies for human preferences and providing scalar rewards for policy optimiza-tion (Christiano et al., 2017). The standard formu-lation follows the Bradley–Terry model (Bradley and Terry, 1952), which assigns scalar scores based on pairwise preference data (Bai et al., 2022; Ganguli et al., 2022). Recent work extends this paradigm with more fine-grained supervision, such as process reward models (PRMs) that provide 2step-level signals (Lightman et al., 2023). To ad-dress the limitations of raw rewards, a related line of work explores reward shaping techniques that modify or augment reward signals to improve train-ing stability, credit assignment, and robustness to reward hacking (Kwon et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2025). Despite these efforts, most RLHF pipelines ulti-mately rely on a single scalar reward derived from the final hidden state, compressing rich internal representations into a point estimate (Wang et al., 2024a). This overlooks the information encoded in RM representations, which capture semantic and preference-aligned relationships among sam-ples (Yang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b). In this work, we instead leverage RM hidden states as reward features to refine advantage estimation.

We formalize the estimation objective in §4.1 and derive its closed-form solution in §4.2. Finally, we show that the refined advantages can be seamlessly integrated into existing group-based RL algorithms in §4.3.

5.1 Datasets and Evaluation 

We evaluate the performance of GraphAE on three widely adopted RLHF benchmarks: Arena-Hard-v0.1 (Li et al., 2024), AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024), and MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023). These benchmarks require the model to generate answers to a wide range of open-ended questions. We follow the standard evaluation protocol of each benchmark. For Arena-Hard-v0.1, we report the win rate (WR) against GPT-4-0314 with the 95% confidence interval (CI), using GPT-4.1 as the judge. For AlpacaEval 2.0, we report both WR and length-controlled win rate (LC WR) against GPT-4-Turbo. For MT-Bench, we report two turns and average scores using GPT-4-Turbo as the judge. More details are provided in the Appendix A. 

5.2 Baselines 

GraphAE is designed as a plug-in advantage esti-mator for group-based RLHF. We apply it to three representative group-based RL algorithms, includ-ing GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), GSPO (Zheng et al., 2025), and RLOO (Ahmadian et al., 2024). For each method, we replace the original scalar reward based advantage estimator with GraphAE while keeping all other components unchanged. We compare GraphAE against RL methods that use scalar rewards for advantage estimation. To avoid the effect of reward regularization, we further include reward smoothing baselines of the form 

˜ri = (1 − α)ri + α¯r, where ¯r denotes the mean reward within each rollout group and α controls the smoothing strength. 2 For a more comprehensive evaluation, we also include classical baselines such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) and the original instruction-tuned models without RL training.

7 Conclusion 

We propose to leverage reward model hidden states as graph signals to compute advantages. By propa-gating rewards over graphs, our method GraphAE captures local correlations, reduces variance, and generalizes group-based RL algorithms. It bridges the gap between scalar reward estimation and struc-tured inference, offering a more information rich, stable, and effective RLHF paradigm. 8Limitations 

GraphAE relies on the quality of the reward model representations to construct the similarity graph. When the RM hidden states contain weak or noisy preference structure, the effectiveness of graph-based propagation may be reduced. In addition, GraphAE introduces a small amount of extra com-putation for graph construction and matrix solving within each rollout group. Although this overhead is lightweight in our experiments, scaling to sub-stantially larger group sizes may require more effi-cient approximations in future work.

C Effect of Generic Reward Smoothing 

To further distinguish GraphAE from generic re-ward regularization, we study the effect of the smoothing coefficient α in the reward smooth base-line ( ˜ri = (1 − α)ri + α¯r) on final performance. The Qwen + GRPO results are shown in Table 5. We observe that a small amount of smoothing brings only limited benefit, while stronger smooth-ing consistently hurts final performance. As α

increases, the scalar rewards within each rollout group become less distinguishable, which weakens useful preference contrasts among responses. This suggests that indiscriminately suppressing reward variance is not sufficient, and can even be harm-ful when the original reward differences contain meaningful preference information. In contrast, GraphAE improves performance substantially over both vanilla GRPO and all re-ward smoothing variants. The key difference is that GraphAE does not collapse all rewards toward the group mean. Instead, it propagates reward in-formation locally according to the similarity graph induced by RM hidden states. Therefore, responses that are close in the RM representation space can share information, while unrelated responses are not forced to receive similar rewards. This selec-tive, structure-aware denoising allows GraphAE to reduce noisy local fluctuations without destroying useful preference distinctions, explaining why it outperforms generic reward smoothing.

E Case Study 

We further provide several qualitative examples to show how GraphAE changes individual rewards. Table 6 reports four Qwen + GRPO rollout cases. Each case contains a target response, its nearest neighbor in the RM hidden space, the original scalar RM reward, and the refined GraphAE re-ward. These examples reveal a clear pattern. The scalar RM sometimes gives a reward that is in-consistent with nearby responses. In the first two cases, the target response receives a relatively high scalar reward, but its nearest neighbors suggest that this reward is over-estimated. GraphAE therefore pulls the reward down. In the last two cases, the target response receives a very low scalar reward, although it is close to higher-reward responses in the RM hidden space. GraphAE then raises its reward toward the local neighborhood. This shows why GraphAE works. It does not simply smooth all rewards toward the group mean. Instead, it uses the RM hidden space to identify which responses should influence each other. If a response is close to strong neighbors, GraphAE can recover an under-estimated reward. If a re-sponse is close to weaker or more cautious neigh-bors, GraphAE can suppress an over-estimated re-ward. In this way, GraphAE reduces local reward noise while keeping the preference structure en-coded by the RM representations.
