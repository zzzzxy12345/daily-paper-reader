# ABSTRACT 

Reinforcement learning for multi-step LLM agents often relies on scalar rewards that indicate success but cannot explain why a trajectory is good or bad. Rubric-based rewards improve interpretability through natural-language criteria, but ex-isting methods score at the trajectory level and freeze the scorer behind a closed-source judge, leaving step-level credit assignment unresolved and the judge itself static. We propose ARCO (Adaptive Rubric CO -evolution), a rubric framework in which a same-scale model μ shares a backbone with two heads: a generation head that produces per-step criteria, and a score head that predicts rubric-conditioned 

step-level rewards. A trajectory decomposition constraint ties the sum of step rewards to the terminal outcome, enabling credit assignment without step-level labels, while μ and the policy π are jointly updated on on-policy data so that the rubric content and the scoring function co-evolve at the parameter level. Across HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and MuSiQue with two open-source backbones, ARCO improves the best EM in every setting over strong outcome-, rubric-, and process-reward baselines, and analyses show that its rubrics are step-specific, robust to design choices, and useful for diagnosing agent behavior. Codes and data are available at https://github.com/zihangtian/ARCO .

# 1 INTRODUCTION 

Reinforcement learning (RL) has become a standard approach for improving multi-step LLM-based agents on tasks such as multi-hop QA and tool use Zeng et al. (2025); Kalyan & Andrews (2025); Jin et al. (2025). Most methods train with black-box scalar rewards—either trajectory-level outcome rewards or step-level process rewards—that indicate quality but cannot explain why a trajectory is good or bad Ouyang et al. (2022); Lightman et al. (2024); Xi et al. (2025). This opacity invites reward hacking and limits the granularity of credit assignment across steps Gao et al. (2023). Rubric-based rewards offer a more interpretable alternative by grounding evaluation in explicit natural-language criteria Gunjal et al. (2025); Gupta et al. (2025); Shao et al. (2025); Liu et al. (2025a). Yet existing rubric methods share two limitations. First, they all operate at the trajectory level: a single rubric scores the entire rollout, leaving the credit-assignment problem at individual steps untouched. Second, they rely on a frozen closed-source LLM (e.g., GPT-4) as the external judge—whether the rubric is static Gunjal et al. (2025), generated dynamically per trajectory Gupta et al. (2025), or evolved through an on-policy buffer Shao et al. (2025), only the rubric text may change while the judge’s parameters never update. Consider a multi-hop question: In what year was the university where Sergei Tokarev was a professor founded? A black-box reward assigns the same score to a trajectory that luckily guesses “1755” and one that methodically searches for Tokarev’s university before looking up its founding year. A static rubric might check “did the agent search?”—but once the agent learns to search, its errors shift to subtler modes: targeting the wrong entity, redundant retrieval, or premature answers. A frozen judge cannot adapt its scoring to these evolving failure modes, regardless of how the rubric text is updated. # 2 PRELIMINARY 

A multi-step LLM-based agent solves a task q ∈ Q by sequentially selecting actions at ∼ π(· | ht)

given a trajectory prefix ht = ( q, a 1, o 1, . . . , a t−1, o t−1), producing a trajectory τ with binary reward 

R(τ ) ∈ { 0, 1}. Rubric-based process supervision augments scalar scoring with natural-language criteria: at each step, a generator ρ produces evaluation criteria and a scorer g predicts a reward conditioned on them: 

rt = ρ(ht, a t), st = g(ht, a t, r t). (1) Existing methods differ in three design axes. Rubric adaptation : criteria may be static (fixed before training) Gunjal et al. (2025); Liu et al. (2025a), dynamically generated per trajectory but with a frozen generator Gupta et al. (2025), or evolving at the content level via on-policy buffer management Shao et al. (2025)—yet in all cases the scoring function’s parameters remain frozen, and criteria are 2Preprint typically shared across all steps. Scoring granularity : g usually produces a single trajectory-level score R(τ ) rather than per-step scores st Gupta et al. (2025); Gunjal et al. (2025). Judge model :existing rubric-based methods rely on closed-source LLMs (e.g. GPT) as external judges, preventing the scorer from co-evolving with the policy. ARCO addresses all three axes: ρ generates context-specific criteria per step using a same-scale model, g predicts step-level scores conditioned on those criteria, and both ρ and g are co-evolved with 

π at the parameter level on on-policy data throughout training. 

# 3 ARCO 

We consider a multi-step LLM-based agent solving instances q ∈ Q . At step t, the policy π observes a trajectory prefix ht=( q, a 1, o 1, . . . , a t−1, o t−1) and selects an action at∼π(· | ht). The environment returns observations for tool actions such as retrieval, and the trajectory τ receives a binary outcome reward R(τ ) ∈ { 0, 1} after the agent finishes or reaches the horizon. ARCO turns this sparse signal into interpretable process supervision by attaching a natural-language rubric rt and a scalar score st

to every action. Unlike pipelines with a frozen rubric writer or judge, ARCO instantiates both rubric generation and scoring inside one trainable model μ that co-evolves with the policy π.3.1 HIERARCHICAL RUBRIC MODEL 

The rubric model μ uses one causal LM backbone fθ with two lightweight heads, separating what should be checked from how well the action satisfies those checks . The hierarchy is motivated by the fact that step quality is structured and operation-dependent: in multi-hop QA, a search may fail by wrong-entity targeting, broad querying, or redundancy, while a finish may fail by missing support or wrong format. A flat scalar PRM must compress these heterogeneous failure modes into one implicit value; ARCO instead writes a local checklist for the current prefix-action pair and scores the action along that checklist, exposing the evaluator’s latent dimensions as readable criteria and narrowing the score head’s prediction problem to a small set of concrete checks. Concretely, the LM head acts as the high-level rubric generator: given (q, h t, a t), it generates an ordered rubric rt=( ct, 1, . . . , c t,K )∼pθ (· | q, h t, a t), where each criterion names one aspect of action quality. Because criteria are generated after observing the trajectory prefix, the rubric can adapt across stages: early searches can be judged by entity targeting and evidence coverage, later searches by whether they resolve the remaining hop, and finish actions by answer support and format. The score head acts as the low-level evaluator: it reads the same state-action context together with the generated checklist and converts those semantic checks into numeric supervision. # 4 EXPERIMENTS 

We evaluate ARCO on three multi-hop QA benchmarks across two open-source backbone families. We organize the analysis around five research questions: • RQ1. Does ARCO outperform existing ORM, rubric ORM, and PRM baselines? • RQ2. Is each of ARCO’s design choices individually necessary? • RQ3. Do ARCO’s rubrics actually point at the action they scored? • RQ4. How sensitive is ARCO to the number of rubric criteria K?• RQ5. How do rubric-model architecture and scale affect policy learning? 4.1 EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 

Datasets. We evaluate on three multi-hop QA benchmarks of increasing reasoning complexity: 

HotpotQA Yang et al. (2018) (2-hop), 2WikiMultiHopQA Ho et al. (2020) (multi-hop compar-ison/bridge), and MuSiQue Trivedi et al. (2022) (compositional, 2–4 hops). For each dataset we use 

2,000 training and 500 evaluation examples and report Exact Match (EM) and token-level F1. 

Agent environment. Following prior work Bo et al. (2024); Tian et al. (2026); Yao et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2025), we adopt a unified multi-turn retrieval-augmented QA environment. At each step, the agent selects Search[query] (top-1 passage from a SimCSE retriever Gao et al. (2021)) or 

Finish[answer] . The search budget is 3 for HotpotQA and 2WikiMultiHopQA and 6 for MuSiQue; the environment returns binary EM as R(τ ) ∈ { 0, 1}. Full interaction protocol, including forced-finish handling and rubric scoring, is given in Algorithm 1 of Appendix A. 

Implementation details. We instantiate ARCO with two same-scale open-source backbones, Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 Yang et al. (2025) and Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct Grattafiori et al. (RQ4) The rubric turns ARCO’s process reward into a readable step-level signal, and the most direct knob over its shape is the width K—how many criteria μ verbalizes per action. Our main experiments use K=3 ; here we ask how this width affects downstream learning. To isolate K, we reuse the HotpotQA policy-warmup trajectories and re-run only the rubric annotation under K ∈ { 1, 3, 5, 7, 9},so any downstream change is attributable to rubric width; for each K we repeat the same μ SFT and RL recipe and select the best-dev-EM checkpoint. We diagnose rubric content by embedding each criterion and running K-Means within every dev step, with distinct themes, duplicate rate, and within-cluster score range computed over the resulting clusters. Increasing K does not monotonically improve ARCO (Figure 3A): EM peaks at 42 .80 for K=3 , drops at K=5 /7, and only partially recovers at K=9 . Distinct themes saturate quickly: the theme ratio falls by roughly half between 

K=3 and K=9 , so nine criteria still cover about three themes on average (Figure 3B). Duplicates therefore dominate at large K (Figure 3C) and are mostly paraphrastic rather than byte-identical (Figure 4). Throughout the main table π and μ share the same base model, entangling the rubric’s contribution with the policy backbone. RQ5 disentangles them and asks whether final policy quality is controlled by the rubric model’s size or family: we fix the policy to Qwen3-4B and vary only the Qwen3-family μ size, then keep the scale comparable and swap the backbone family in (π, μ ) pairings on HotpotQA. # 5 RELATED WORK 

Reward modeling for multi-step LLM agents. RL for multi-step LLM agents typically uses scalar rewards from one of two families. Outcome reward models (ORMs) inherit trajectory-level feedback from RLHF/DPO Ouyang et al. (2022); Rafailov et al. (2023) and, in agent settings, train end-to-end with binary task success as the only signal Jin et al. (2025); Song et al. (2025); Chen et al. (2026); Zhao et al. (2025); Zeng et al. (2025). Such signals are too coarse for credit assignment across steps and are prone to reward hacking Gao et al. (2023). Process reward models (PRMs) score every step: starting from math step verifiers Lightman et al. (2024), recent agent variants estimate step values via TD/GAE Xi et al. (2025); Choudhury (2025) or via tree-search, step-wise PPO, and implicit step supervision Xiong et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2025a;b); Liu et al. (2025b). These methods address granularity but produce opaque scalars that do not explain why a step is good or bad. 

Rubric-based rewards and joint policy–reward learning. To improve interpretability, rubric-based rewards ground evaluation in natural-language criteria. RaR Gunjal et al. (2025) freezes a query-specific rubric before RL; CARMO Gupta et al. (2025) generates rubrics at scoring time but freezes the generator; OpenRubrics Liu et al. (2025a), Rubric Anchors Huang et al. (2025b), Chasing-the-Tail Zhang et al. (2025), and Online Rubrics Elicitation Rezaei et al. (2025) focus on synthesizing or eliciting rubrics; RLER Shao et al. (2025) evolves a rubric buffer during training; RubricARM Xu et al. (2026) adds alternating training. All score at the trajectory level with a frozen closed-source judge . A separate thread jointly trains policy and reward model Hong et al. (2025); Shi et al. (2025); Huang et al. (2025a); Wang et al. (2026a;b), but uses opaque scalars or no step-level supervision. 

ARCO unifies these axes: it generates step-level natural-language rubrics with a same-scale open-source model, predicts step-level scores, and—through trajectory decomposition—co-evolves rubric content and scoring with the policy at the parameter level , with no external judge. 

# 6 LIMITATIONS 

ARCO trains the rubric model with relatively lightweight objectives. The trajectory decomposition loss only ties the sum of step scores to the terminal outcome, and rubric text is shaped primarily by warmup imitation and KL regularization; richer process supervision or rubric-consistency losses could yield sharper per-step credit. The pace of π–μ co-evolution is also set by fixed learning rates and regularization weights, leaving adaptive update ratios and drift-aware scheduling to future work. Finally, training both π and μ and invoking μ at every active step makes ARCO more expensive than single-model RL, and this overhead grows with horizon length; distillation, rubric caching, or asynchronous μ updates are promising ways to reduce it. These limitations point in the same direction: tighter, more adaptive coupling between the policy and an interpretable evaluator, without giving up the cost-efficiency of an open-source same-scale design. 

# 7 CONCLUSION 

We presented ARCO, an adaptive rubric co-evolution framework for multi-step language agents with interpretable step-level process rewards. ARCO uses a hierarchical rubric model with a shared backbone to generate state-action-specific criteria and score actions under those criteria, while trajectory decomposition lets the evaluator and policy be jointly optimized from terminal outcomes. Because rubric generation, scoring, and policy improvement update the same backbone on the same on-policy rollouts, ARCO turns sparse outcome rewards into a dense, readable, step-aware signal without any external judge. Across multi-hop QA benchmarks and backbones, ARCO improves over outcome- and process-reward baselines, and analyses show that its rubrics are step-specific, robust to design choices, and useful for diagnosing agent behavior. We hope ARCO offers a foundation for adaptive, interpretable, process-aware reward modeling for language agents. 9Preprint

# B WARMUP STAGE DETAILS 

ARCO uses a supervised warmup before on-policy co-evolution so that both the policy and the rubric model start from a working initialization. We collect warmup records by rolling out an API policy on training questions in the same retrieval environment used for RL. For each question we sample multiple trajectories and log every action, observation, parse status, search budget, and terminal reward, then annotate the complete trajectory with a strong rubric model. HotpotQA and 2Wiki use a search horizon of three steps, MuSiQue uses six; the terminal reward is the task EM reward used by the main experiments. 13 Preprint 

Policy warmup. The policy SFT dataset is built only from high-reward trajectories, filtered by a minimum reward threshold ( 1.0 in our main runs) and converted into one supervised example per step. The input contains the question, the trajectory prefix before the current action, and the remaining search budget; when the budget is exhausted and the dataset supports forced finishing, the prompt switches to a finish-only template. The target is the recorded action string, e.g., Search[query] or 

Finish[answer] , which initializes π to imitate successful tool-use traces rather than all sampled behavior.
