Abstract 

Agentic AI systems powered by large language models (LLMs) are rapidly evolving into au-tonomous decision-making systems, exposing attack vectors beyond those of traditional LLM vulnerabilities. Existing security evaluations are often tied to specific implementations or do-mains, limiting unified comparison across het-erogeneous systems. To address this gap, we introduce RIFT-Bench, a graph representation-driven methodology for dynamic red-teaming that enables unified evaluations across diverse agentic architectures. Building on a novel hier-archical representation, RIFT-Bench operates in two automated phases: Discovery , which ex-tracts system structure, and Scanning , which deploys adaptive adversarial attacks and pro-duces a comprehensive evaluation report. It evaluates the examined system itself, leverag-ing a broad set of dynamically adaptable ad-versarial probes across diverse attack vectors and objectives. We demonstrate the effective-ness of the proposed evaluation pipeline across 45 agentic systems spanning a diverse range of implementations, showing that the approach generalizes effectively to heterogeneous agen-tic architectures. Beyond systems and attacks, RIFT-Bench also supports direct evaluation of mitigation strategies. These key capabilities make RIFT-Bench a scalable foundation for security evaluation of agentic AI systems. 

1 Introduction 

Agentic systems powered by large language mod-els (LLMs) are rapidly transitioning from research prototypes to deployed applications. They sup-port multi-step reasoning, tool use, memory, and coordination across domains such as software en-gineering and enterprise automation (Yao et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023; Plaat et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025). While they inherit known LLM vul-nerabilities, including prompt injection, data exfil-tration, and jailbreak-based policy bypass (Perez

Recent work further high-lights system-level threats such as goal hijacking, tool misuse, privilege escalation, memory poison-ing, and insecure coordination (OWASP Agentic Security Initiative, 2025; Ferrag et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025; Ghosh et al., 2025; MITRE Corpora-tion, 2026; Franklin et al., 2026). Together, these properties expand the attack vector beyond stan-dalone LLM deployments, emphasizing the need for agentic AI-suited security evaluation. The rapid adoption of agentic systems has ex-posed a notable lack of standardization, as imple-mentations vary across frameworks, architectures, and coding practices. While emerging efforts such as tool-calling interfaces (OpenAI, 2023), Model Context Protocol (MCP) (Anthropic, 2024), and Agent-to-Agent (A2A) (Google, 2024) communi-cation protocol aim to define shared interaction patterns, the implementation of the agentic systems remains highly heterogeneous. This variability hin-ders interoperability and prevents comparable eval-uation across diverse systems (Next Moca, 2025). Recent work has begun to address these chal-lenges through agentic red-teaming tools, security benchmarks, and evaluation environments (Zenity, 2024; Debenedetti et al., 2024; An et al., 2026; Franklin et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2026). How-ever, existing approaches often remain tied to implementation-specific patterns, simulated envi-ronments or interaction protocols, limiting direct evaluation across real-world systems. Above all, the lack of standardization prevents attack sets from being directly reused across heterogeneous agen-tic systems or varying agentic implementations, limiting scalable evaluation beyond the tested im-plementations and setups. To address these gaps, we introduce RIFT-Bench, a representation-driven methodology for dynami-cal red-teaming of agentic systems. The approach is based on a key insight that adversarial eval-uation can be made transferable through a uni-fied representation of the examined system’s struc-ture. Specifically, RIFT-Bench builds on Node-Spec, a novel standardized hierarchical representa-tion that captures agentic components, interactions, and implementation-level grounding. To demonstrate the correctness and applicability of RIFT-Bench, we build a benchmark of 45 diverse agentic systems spanning multiple frameworks and architectures. We also create over 100 adversar-ial probes that instantiate into more than 10,000 distinct attack tests across the benchmark, demon-strating scalable vulnerability assessment across diverse agentic system designs. We further demon-strate how mitigation strategies can be evaluated under realistic attack scenarios using the proposed method. This positions RIFT-Bench as a solid foun-dation for dynamic, holistic, and agnostic security evaluation of agentic systems. Given the breadth of RIFT-Bench, spanning rep-resentation, discovery, scanning, tool emulation, benchmark construction, and evaluation, we pro-vide extensive algorithmic, implementation, and evaluation details in the rich appendix. 

Our main contributions are as follows :(i) We propose NodeSpec, a code-driven hierarchi-cal representation that links agentic components to their implementation code blocks, improving read-ability and enabling targeted system modifications. (ii) We develop an automated Discovery pipeline that extracts NodeSpec from given implementa-tions and supports safe, cost-aware tool emulation. (iii) We introduce a scalable Scanning module for structure-aware probe selection and attack instanti-ation, trace-based evaluation, and defense integra-tion directly on the examined agentic system. (iv) We instantiate these components in RIFT-Bench, an implementation-agnostic infrastructure for agentic security evaluation, and further intro-duce a benchmark suite of 45 heterogeneous agen-tic systems and 105 adversarial probes. Upon ac-ceptance, RIFT-Bench and benchmark artifacts will be released to the AI security community. 22 Background and Related Work 

Agentic systems. LLM-based agents extend LLM capabilities through tool use, memory, and coor-dination, enabling multi-step task execution (Yao et al., 2022; Schick et al., 2023; MIT, 2025). This shift is reflected in orchestration frameworks (Mi-crosoft, 2023; LangChain, 2023; crewAI, 2025), production SDKs for agent loops and tracing (Za-haria et al., 2018; OpenAI, 2024b; Anthropic, 2025b), and emerging protocols such as MCP, A2A, and Agent Skills (Anthropic, 2024; Google, 2024; Anthropic, 2025a). These systems increasingly dif-fer not only in model choice, but also in how tools, memory, control flow, and agent interactions are implemented. This diversity of implementations motivates a standardized representation of agentic system structure for unified behavioral analysis. 

System representations. Agentic vulnerabilities often emerge from interactions among untrusted in-puts, tools, memory, and privileged actions (Beurer-Kellner et al., 2025; Deng et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025), making structural understanding of the un-derlying agentic implementation essential for secu-rity evaluation. Existing specification-driven ap-proaches model agent workflows (Amini et al., 2025; Next Moca, 2025; Gao et al., 2025), while observability platforms trace runtime behavior and detect anomalies in multi-agent systems (Gupta et al., 2025; Solomon et al., 2025; Waknin et al., 2026). However, these efforts primarily support specification or monitoring, rather than automated structural analysis for portable adversarial evalua-tion. 

Agentic security evaluation. LLM security eval-uation has evolved around automated red team-ing, jailbreak attacks, and prompt injections (Perez et al., 2022; Zou et al., 2023; Sha and Zhang, 2024), with scanners and frameworks supporting large-scale prompt robustness testing (Derczynski et al., 2024; Microsoft, 2024; IBM, 2024; Webster, 2024; Brokman et al., 2025). These methods assume text-in and text-out endpoints, which are insufficient for assessing agentic systems that expose risks through external resources, private data, and environment-modifying actions (Franklin et al., 2026). Recent agent-specific benchmarks embed at-tacks in tool-integrated or stateful environ-ments (Debenedetti et al., 2024; Zhan et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025; Vijayvargiya et al., 2026). A dominant recent example is DTap (Chen et al., 2026), which provides a con-trollable red-teaming platform with a large-scale set of simulated environments and attack instances. On the other hand, commercial platforms provide agent-focused scanning or runtime monitoring via black-box interaction or protocol-specific inspec-tions (Pillar Security, 2024; Zenity, 2024; Lakera AI, 2025; Cisco AI Defense, 2025; Akto, 2026). While these efforts move beyond prompt-only test-ing, they largely remain tied to specific simulated environments or deployment assumptions, which limit their applicability (see Appendix A). Unified benchmarking efforts (Ye et al., 2025; An et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2026) evaluate multiple agent frameworks through shared interfaces and ab-stractions. However, they often require manually adapting each system to the benchmark interface, adding substantial setup effort and shifting evalua-tion away from the original implementation. As a result, attack suites are difficult to transfer to new examined systems without manual integration. 

3 Problem Formulation 

We consider an adversary who can influence either the user prompt, the environment, or both. The environment includes tools, memory, external and internal resources, and inter-component communi-cation channels. The adversary may pursue diverse goals, including altering the final output, inducing unintended actions, modifying the environment, or disrupting the system’s execution. Appendix B fur-ther contrasts this with LLM red-teaming, where the adversarial surface is limited to the prompt and the observed final text response. 

3.1 Taxonomy 

We decompose agentic red-teaming along two or-thogonal axes: attack surface and adversarial ob-jective (see Fig. 2). The attack surface captures 

where and how adversarial influence is inserted into the agentic system, such as user inputs, tools, memory, or external resources. The adversarial objective captures what goal the attacker aims to achieve. Together, these axes support modular eval-uation across attack mechanisms and induced fail-ures, and align with cybersecurity taxonomies (Al-Sada et al., 2024; Vassilev et al., 2024; MITRE Corporation, 2026). See an extended mapping to established security taxonomies in Appendix C. 

Attack Surface. We define the attack surface as a four-level hierarchy, capturing how an adversary’s influence is integrated into the agentic systems. 3Figure 2: Our proposed attack taxonomy: a hierarchical attack-surface axis organizes how adversarial influence is introduced, while an objective axis labels the intended failure outcome, allowing the same attack to be instanti-ated with different objectives. 

This hierarchy organizes the attack space from broad entry points to executable, system-specific attack instances. ( i) Surface denotes the entry-point class (e.g., tools, memory, external resources, user input); ( ii ) Suite specifies an operational config-uration within a surface (e.g., injection via tool outputs); ( iii ) Probe is a reusable, system-agnostic, adversarial template with defined activation condi-tions, paired with evaluators; ( iv ) Attempt is a con-crete, system-specific instantiation of a probe. This hierarchy makes the attack space explicit, enabling attacks to be organized, reused, and evaluated for coverage across different agentic systems. For example, a malicious tool surface may in-clude description-level injection, unauthorized ac-tions and output manipulation as distinct suites ;within each suite different probes define reusable templates such as instructing the agent to invoke an-other tool; and each probe can yield many attempts 

by instantiating it on different tools and arguments. 

Adversarial Objective. Adversarial objectives specify the type of failure the attacker aims to trigger in the agentic system. We consider four representative categories that illustrate common failure outcomes: ( i) Perform unwanted actions 

(unintended or irrelevant actions), ( ii ) Disrupt task completion (failure to complete the task), ( iii ) Leak internal information (exposure of internal or sys-tem data), and ( iv ) Resource overload (excessive computational or financial cost). 

4 NodeSpec: System Representation 

The structural diversity of agentic systems makes standardization essential for consistent vulnerabil-ity analysis. To address this challenge, we intro-duce NodeSpec , a hierarchical representation that captures an agentic system as a set of components. The agentic system is represented as a nested graph, where each component corresponds to a node with metadata and can recursively contain other nodes, capturing both local components and higher-level system structure. NodeSpec provides a unified rep-resentation of agentic systems and is designed to support systematic modification of system compo-nents across heterogeneous implementations. Each node is characterized along five dimen-sions. Identity captures core identifiers such as node name and type. Provenance links nodes to implementation artifacts, including code references and descriptive context. Topology encodes struc-tural relationships, including child components and connectivity between nodes. Interface and con-figuration specify inputs and outputs and key con-figuration elements such as prompts or required resources. Capabilities capture critical behavioral properties, such as code execution and data access permissions. Appendix D provides the full field list and a detailed NodeSpec example. A key design principle of NodeSpec is that it is implementation-grounded . Code references, prompts and configuration values preserve direct links to the underlying implementation. This en-ables systematic probe adaptation and controlled manipulation of specific system components. 

5 RIFT-Bench: Methodology 

RIFT-Bench operates through two phases. First, 

Discovery analyzes the system implementation and generates a code-driven representation (NodeSpec). Based on this representation, the Scanning phase adaptively selects and instantiates applicable ad-versarial probes as attack attempts tailored to the examined system. The system’s behavior is then evaluated from the resulting execution traces. This process enables evaluation of both agentic systems and attack probes within a scalable, systematic pipeline. 5.1.1 Structure Identifier 

The Structure Identifier (SI) is a modular four-stage pipeline that combines LLM-based analysis with deterministic validation logic to construct an exam-ined system’s NodeSpec representation. Pipeline is illustrated in Fig. 1 and briefly described below. A detailed algorithm is provided in Appendix I.1. 

Resource Initialization. Given the examined sys-tem’s code implementation and an example execu-tion command, the pipeline initializes the resources required for subsequent steps. These resources in-clude a file index and a retrieval database over the codebase, a NodeSpec database of possible nodes, a root node (root of the representation graph), and a short textual system summary. Collectively, these artifacts provide the contextual evidence required to support the subsequent stages. 

Dynamic Graph Construction. The graph rep-resentation of the system is constructed iteratively from the root node using modular components such as node creation, child discovery, attachment, re-moval, and subgraph connectivity. Each compo-nent performs a targeted operation, enabling reuse and iterative refinement of the graph. This mod-ular design supports robust extraction under high structural variance, allowing adaptation to diverse implementations and system architectures. 

Graph Validation. The graph is refined through static validation, graph correctness checks, and dy-namic (execution-based) validation. These steps reduce missed components by revisiting candidate nodes from the full catalog (static validation), en-forcing structural consistency (graph correctness), and grounding the representation in observed exe-cution traces (dynamic validation).

5.1.2 Tool Emulation 

Unlike standalone LLMs, agentic systems are ex-plicitly designed to interact with internal and exter-nal services through tool calls. Thus, security eval-uation must account for tool-mediated interactions to accurately reflect real-world deployment condi-tions (Vijayvargiya et al., 2026). However, native tool execution can result in unauthorized access to private data, high operational cost, and tool-level side effects that may confound assessment. To address this, we support configurable tool em-ulation, allowing selective substitution of tools with emulated counterparts. This capability is optional 

and provides a safe, controllable, and cost-aware setting. Our approach adopts the commonly used LLM-based emulation paradigm (Ruan et al., 2023) conditioned on structured tool specifications and usage examples derived from the NodeSpec repre-sentation. Implementation details and evaluation of emulation fidelity are provided in Appendix E. 

5.2 Scanning Phase 

Given the extracted system representation, the scan-ning phase evaluates the agentic system through system-specific attack attempts. Each attempt is instantiated from a reusable probe, integrated into the examined system, and executed to measure its effect on system behavior. This enables systematic assessment of the system’s vulnerabilities. 

5.2.1 Probes and evaluators 

A probe is a reusable, system-agnostic adversarial template that defines both the structure of an attack and the mechanism through which it is injected into an agentic system (Derczynski et al., 2024). To enable system-specific adaptation, probes contain 5placeholders that are instantiated using the Node-Spec representation to generate concrete attack at-tempts. Each probe is additionally associated with a set of predefined requirements that determine its applicability to different agentic systems based on their structure and capabilities (e.g., presence of tools, memory, or specific capabilities); conse-quently, only valid probes are selected for a given examined system. Full details are in Appendix G.1. To assess the impact of the generated attacks, probes also define associated evaluators , which measure system behavior under attack using met-rics such as attack activation, attack success, exe-cution drift, and, when applicable, task utility (see Sec. 6.3 for definitions). Since different attacks target different behavioral properties and attack surfaces, each probe is paired with a subset of eval-uators aligned with its specific objective. Results from these evaluators, collected across multiple sur-faces and objectives, can later be incorporated into a broader risk assessment (NIST, 2012) that con-siders factors such as attack likelihood, outcome impact, and the importance of the affected compo-nent (Giloni et al., 2025). 

5.2.2 Probe Filtering 

Before scanning, probes are filtered to ensure ap-plicability to the given agentic system based on their predefined requirements, which are matched against the NodeSpec. This design enables fully deterministic filtering, selecting only probes whose requirements are satisfied. The resulting subset ensures that generated attack attempts target mean-ingful system behaviors rather than architectural mismatches. 

5.2.3 Attack Execution Pipeline 

After probe filtering, scanning proceeds through four main steps, (see Fig. 1) detailed below. (i) Attempts Generation. Probe placeholders are populated by the NodeSpec representation, gen-erating attack attempts that target specific system components. A single probe can yield multiple at-tempts by instantiating placeholders with different system elements (e.g., available tools or agents). (ii) Agentic System Adaptation. Each attack at-tempt is integrated into the examined system to inject adversarial behavior, with the integration pro-cess guided by NodeSpec to enable architecture-agnostic adaptation. (iii) Execution and Tracing. Each attack is executed independently in a sandboxed environment, ensur-ing isolation and controlled experimentation, while tracing mechanisms capture execution logs. (iv) Trace Evaluation. Finally, the execution traces are evaluated using the probe’s associated evalua-tors, providing a multi-faceted assessment of attack effectiveness and system behavior. 

5.3 RIFT-Bench Modularity 

RIFT-Bench is designed as a modular evaluation pipeline with decoupled components for represen-tation extraction, probe generation, attack instan-tiation, execution, tracing, and evaluation. Each component exposes well-defined interfaces, allow-ing modules to be independently extended or re-placed. This supports rapid experimentation across agentic systems and continuous integration of new components. For example, the deterministic probe filtering described in Sec. 5.2.2 can be replaced with more sophisticated filtering strategies.

6.3 Evaluators 

Each probe is associated with a set of evaluators, where each evaluator measures a single metric. Ide-ally, given oracle indicators, every probe would in-clude a dedicated evaluator for each metric. Since such indicators are often unavailable in practice, our framework supports multiple evaluators per metric, enabling comparison of alternative mea-surement strategies. In our benchmark, for exam-ple, we employ both deterministic and LLM-based evaluators for attack success. Overall, the bench-mark includes 23 evaluators spanning four metrics: 

attack activation , attack success , task utility , and 

execution drift .

Attack Activation captures whether the execu-tion actually engages the surface that carries the attack (e.g., calling the poisoned tool, retrieving the malicious chunk, or invoking the agent with a compromised system prompt). Attack Success then captures whether this activated influence achieves the attacker’s objective, such as triggering an unau-thorized tool invocation. When ground-truth tasks are available, we report 

Task Utility , which measures whether the system completes the intended task correctly. This is im-portant because attack success alone does not cap-ture whether the agent remained useful or whether the task was disrupted. Task Utility evaluates the correctness of the system’s output, environment state changes, and tool invocations, thereby provid-ing a direct assessment of task completion. Finally, Execution Drift (ED) measures the be-havioral divergence between benign and adversar-ial executions, providing a robustness signal when ground-truth outcomes are unavailable. Notably, the observed negative correlation between ED and utility suggests that it serves as an effective proxy for behavioral disruption in such settings (see Ap-pendix J). We define ED as: ED = α ∆comp + β ∆out (1) where ∆comp denotes component-level execution drift, ∆out denotes final-output drift, α, β ∈ [0 , 1] 

with α + β = 1 . ED captures a broad range of adversarial effects, including altered tool usage, execution rerouting, output modification, and com-plete execution blockage. Additional details on the implemented evaluators are provided in Appendix G. 

6.4 Defenses 

Existing defenses against adversarial manipulation usually operate at one of two levels: the compo-nent level, where safeguards are embedded within individual modules (e.g., LLM-based input/output filtering (Inan et al., 2023; Rachmil et al., 2025)), and the system level, where the orchestration logic between agents and tools is modified to reduce vul-nerability (Debenedetti et al., 2025; Betser et al., 2026). Mirroring the probe integration process, de-fenders can define custom intervention logic that is automatically incorporated into the examined agentic system, enabling systematic evaluation of defensive strategies within our framework. The benchmark includes a simple description removal defense, which strips tool descriptions prior to execution, and incorporates two defenses from AgentDojo (Debenedetti et al., 2024): prompt sandwiching and data delimiters . Beyond these defenses, the extensible design of RIFT-Bench fa-cilitates the integration and evaluation of newly proposed defense mechanisms, enabling scalable analysis of the trade-offs between adversarial ro-bustness, computational overhead, and task utility.

Full results, additional details, and analyses are provided in Appendix J. 

8 Discussion 

Agentic systems challenge traditional LLM red-teaming assumptions because their security behav-ior is shaped not only by the underlying model, but also by the surrounding execution environment: the tools they can invoke, the data they access and the way their components interact. As a result, evaluating such systems requires moving beyond static, framework-specific attacks and toward meth-ods that can reason about system behavior across diverse implementations. Our results in Section 7.2 suggest that structural representations provide a practical path toward this goal. By abstracting an agentic system into NodeSpec, RIFT-Bench can ground adversarial probes in the system’s func-tional structure rather than in the details of a spe-cific framework, implementation, or protocol. This enables attack strategies to become more transfer-able, while still allowing them to be instantiated in a way that is relevant to the target system. This abstraction is especially important because agentic systems are not fixed targets. They often operate in open-ended environments, where tasks, available resources, and execution traces may vary significantly across deployments. In this setting, fixed benchmarks can provide only a partial view of robustness. A benchmark can capture whether a system fails on a predefined set of attacks, but it may miss vulnerabilities that emerge from the particular structure, tools, and workflows of a new system. Dynamically instantiating attacks from the discovered NodeSpec representation allows the evaluation to adapt alongside the target system. This enables the evaluation to focus on attacks that are relevant to the target system, adapt them to its specific components, and analyze how their effects propagate throughout execution. This also changes how robustness should be mea-sured. In conventional LLM evaluation, attack suc-cess is often associated with an undesirable final output (see Appendix B for more details). For agen-tic systems, however, the final answer alone may hide important failures. Attacks can be stealthy and avoid visibly harming system functionality, meaning that a system may still appear to complete the task successfully even when the attack has in-fluenced its execution (evidenced in Appendix J). Evaluating only the final outcome may therefore overlook important effects of an attack. To ad-dress this, we analyze the full execution trace and monitor events throughout the system. The trace al-lows us to validate whether the attack was actually activated during execution, and when needed, to measure attack success internally rather than rely-ing only on the final response. It also enables us to 9evaluate system performance more reliably, either through task success (using the expected outcome), or by comparing the attacked execution against the corresponding benign execution. At the same time, there is a natural tradeoff be-tween generality and specificity. Our framework is designed to support evaluation across heteroge-neous agentic systems, and therefore it relies on probes that can be reused across different archi-tectures. This makes the approach useful for an initial robustness assessment against common ad-versarial threats. However, in some cases, system-specific probes may provide additional insight. In this sense, our framework is not meant to replace tailored red-teaming completely, but to provide a reusable starting point for it. The predefined probes can first help identify common weaknesses through the discovered NodeSpec representation, and prac-titioners can then extend the evaluation with more specialized probes or adversarial adjustments while still using the same structural abstraction. Taken together, these points suggest that scalable agentic red-teaming requires a reusable structural basis, attacks that can be adjusted to each target sys-tem, and evaluation that is grounded in the system’s full execution. 

9 Conclusions 

We present RIFT-Bench, a dynamic red-teaming framework for system-level security evaluation of agentic AI systems. By introducing NodeSpec, a code-grounded representation, RIFT-Bench en-ables reusable probes to be adapted and executed directly on examined systems across diverse imple-mentations. Our benchmark instantiates with 45 agentic systems and 105 probes and 3 mitigation strategies within a scalable pipeline that is open to extension by the AI community. Together, these components provide an implementation-aware eval-uation setup for studying how agentic systems be-have under adversarial conditions. Looking forward, several directions can further broaden the applicability of this framework. In particular, extending structure-aware evaluation be-yond the current white-box setting to gray-box and black-box scenarios would increase its practical rel-evance. Additional opportunities include support for multimodal agentic systems and attack surfaces, expansion of probe coverage to emerging threats, and deeper investigation of evaluator design and its influence on vulnerability assessment. We hope RIFT-Bench serves as a foundation for future re-search on scalable and transferable security evalua-tion of increasingly complex agentic systems. 

Limitations 

While RIFT-Bench represents progress toward vul-nerability scanning for agentic systems, several limitations remain. First, RIFT-Bench operates in a white-box setting and assumes access to the ex-amined system’s implementation code. Second, it currently relies on an existing tracing mechanism (e.g., MLflow), introducing a partial dependency on an observability stack that may require adaptation for some systems. Finally, while RIFT-Bench sup-ports multiple evaluator mechanisms and success criteria, conclusions may depend on the selected evaluators and their underlying assumptions. 

Ethical Considerations 

RIFT-Bench is designed to support defensive secu-rity evaluation of agentic AI systems. Because the benchmark includes adversarial probes and attack templates, releasing it requires careful measures to reduce the risk of misuse. We therefore frame probes as evaluation artifacts, emphasize controlled and sandboxed execution, and support tool emula-tion to avoid unintended side effects on external services or private data. The framework should be used only on systems where the evaluator has authorization. By enabling systematic testing and mitigation strategies evaluation, RIFT-Bench aims to improve the safety and robustness of deployed agentic systems.

A Additional Comparison to Agentic Security Evaluation 

Existing agentic security evaluation efforts can be broadly grouped into three categories: agent-specific benchmarks, unified benchmarking plat-forms, and commercial scanning or monitoring tools. These efforts move beyond prompt-only LLM evaluation, but differ from RIFT-Bench in three key aspects: whether they evaluate the orig-inal examined system’s implementation, whether they automatically discover system structure, and whether attacks are adapted to each examined sys-tem. 

Agent-specific benchmarks. Recent bench-marks evaluate agents in tool-integrated or stateful environments (Ruan et al., 2023; Debenedetti et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Zhan et al., 2024; Levy et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2025; Dong et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025; Yin et al., 2026; Vijayvargiya et al., 2026; Li et al., 2026; Zheng et al., 2026; 14 Evtimov et al., 2026; Hofman et al., 2025). These benchmarks are important because they capture risks that arise from tool use, external resources, state, and multi-step execution. However, they typically define the environment and task interface as part of the benchmark itself. As a result, attacks are evaluated within controlled benchmark environments rather than being automatically adapted to an arbitrary agentic system implemen-tation. For example, DTap (Chen et al., 2026) provides a large controllable red-teaming platform with many simulated environments and attack instances, but the evaluation remains centered on platform-defined simulations rather than structural discovery of a new target codebase. 

Unified benchmark interfaces. Unified bench-marking efforts (Ye et al., 2025; An et al., 2026; Ma et al., 2026) support comparison across multiple agent frameworks by exposing shared interfaces or abstractions. This improves comparability, but of-ten requires manually adapting each system to the benchmark interface. Such adaptation shifts evalu-ation away from the original implementation and makes attack suites harder to transfer directly to new systems without additional manual integration. 

Commercial scanners and runtime monitors. 

Commercial and open-source tools increasingly provide agent discovery, posture management, MCP inspection, guardrails, and runtime monitor-ing (Straiker, 2024; Pillar Security, 2024; Troj.AI, 2024; Zenity, 2024; Noma Security, 2024; Lakera AI, 2025; Agentic Radar, 2025; Cisco AI Defense, 2025; Akto, 2026). These systems are valuable for operational security, especially for identifying deployed agents, monitoring runtime behavior, or detecting risky tool and MCP configurations. How-ever, their discovery (according to publicly open resources) is generally asset- or protocol-oriented rather than a code-grounded structural extraction of the target agentic system. They also do not gen-erally provide reusable adversarial probes that are automatically instantiated through the discovered system structure and executed as system-specific attack attempts. 

Positioning of RIFT-Bench. In contrast, RIFT-Bench combines three capabilities that are usually treated separately. First, the Discovery phase ex-tracts a code-grounded NodeSpec representation of the examined system. Second, reusable probes are filtered and instantiated using this represen-tation, producing system-specific attack attempts. Third, the resulting attacks are executed directly on the examined system’s implementation and evalu-ated through execution traces. This combination enables scalable evaluation of new systems, new probes, and mitigation strategies without requiring manual re-implementation of the system inside a fixed benchmark environment. 

B Structural Differences Between LLM and Agentic Red-Teaming 

This section expands the formal distinction in-troduced in Sec. 3. Standard LLM red-teaming typically evaluates a single model in a prompt-response setting. The adversary supplies or modi-fies a prompt p, the model produces a text response 

t = f (p), and vulnerability existence is determined by applying a predicate to the prompt-response pair. This formulation is appropriate when the main ob-servable behavior is the generated text. Agentic systems require a broader formulation. The evaluated object is not only a model, but a structured system SF implemented using some framework or execution environment F. Such sys-tems may include multiple agents, tools, memory stores, external resources, orchestration logic, local state, and runtime configuration. As a result, the ad-versarial surface is not limited to the prompt. An ad-versary may influence the user input, but may also manipulate environment elements such as retrieved documents, tool outputs, local resources, memory entries, tool descriptions, or inter-component mes-sages. This changes the observed behavior used for evaluation. In an LLM-only setting, the final text response is often the primary evidence of success or failure. In an agentic system, important failures may occur before the final response is produced, or may not be visible in the final response at all. For example, an agent may invoke an unintended tool, modify state, leak information through an interme-diate component, or enter an expensive execution loop while still producing a benign-looking final answer. Therefore, agentic red-teaming must evalu-ate the full execution trace, including intermediate decisions, tool calls, state changes, component in-teractions, and final outputs. C Mapping to Established Security Taxonomies 

Our taxonomy is designed to align with established security taxonomies while adding the structure needed for agentic-system evaluation. In partic-ular, MITRE ATLAS (MITRE Corporation, 2026) organizes adversarial behavior into tactics and tech-niques, where tactics describe adversarial goals and techniques describe the mechanisms used to achieve them. Our adversarial objectives corre-spond most closely to tactic-level goals, while our 

attack surfaces and suites correspond to technique-or sub-technique-level mechanisms. For example, the MITRE ATLAS LLM Prompt Injection technique includes direct, indirect, and triggered prompt-injection variants. Our suites re-fine this idea for agentic systems by distinguish-ing the system pathway through which the injec-tion is introduced. Thus, Prompt Injection and 

Multi-Turn Prompt Injection correspond to user-facing injection paths, Injection in Resource Con-tent corresponds to indirect injection through ex-ternal resources, Agent-Tool Injection captures in-jection through tool-mediated communication, and 

Backdoor Activation captures triggered behavior in which an attack is inserted through one surface and activated through another. More broadly, our attack suites can be viewed as agentic sub-techniques: they specify not only the adversarial mechanism, but also the component or interaction pathway through which it is real-ized. For instance, Memory Poisoning and Local Resource Poisoning instantiate state-manipulation attacks; System Prompt Injection and Description-Level Injection target configuration or component-level instructions; and Unauthorized Action Exe-cution captures attacks that induce unsafe or unin-tended tool use. This mapping allows RIFT-Bench to retain compatibility with high-level security tax-onomies while providing finer-grained coverage for system-level agentic evaluation. Our four adversarial objectives primarily align with two high-level MITRE-style tactics: Exfiltra-tion and Impact . Leak internal information directly maps to Exfiltration, as the attacker aims to expose internal, private, or system-level data. Perform unwanted actions , Disrupt task completion , and 

Resource overload align most closely with Impact, since they aim to alter system behavior, prevent task completion, or degrade operational availabil-ity. More generally, all of our attacks can also be viewed as instances of Execution , because each probe seeks to induce an unintended behavior or action within the agentic system.

E.1 Emulation Goals 

We evaluate tool emulation along three dimensions. 

Semantic fidelity measures whether the emulated output is consistent with the tool documentation and invocation arguments. Behavioral realism mea-sures whether the output resembles the real tool be-havior, including formatting, error messages, and edge cases. Robustness measures whether the emu-lator handles malformed inputs, invalid arguments, and failure cases in a way that is consistent with the original tool. These goals are aligned with prior work on emu-lated and simulated tool environments. ToolEmu evaluates whether emulated tools are accurate and consistent with actual tool execution, including proper handling of invalid inputs (Ruan et al., 2023). SynthTools separates parameter validation from response generation and evaluates schema failures, constraint failures, successful executions with known inputs, and successful executions with new inputs (Castellani et al., 2025). Mir-rorAPI evaluates simulated API responses using documentation-following and similarity to real re-sponses (Guo et al., 2025). GTM evaluates correct-ness, consistency, helpfulness, and usefulness of simulated tool behavior (Ren et al., 2025). 

E.2 Emulation Procedure 

For the emulation prompt and evaluator setup, we build on the MirrorAPI protocol (Guo et al., 2025). Specifically, our emulator prompt is based on the paper’s API-server formulation, where the model receives the tool documentation and invocation re-quest and is instructed to return a structured re-sponse consistent with the tool’s intended behavior. For each target tool, we generate both valid and invalid invocation samples. Valid samples are gen-erated from the tool name, description, expected inputs, and other available tool metadata. Invalid samples include both schema-level invalidity, such as missing required arguments or incorrect argu-ment types, and logic-level invalidity, such as val-ues that satisfy the schema but violate tool-specific constraints. We then execute both the original tool and the emulated tool on each input. The original tool output is treated as the reference behavior for fidelity evaluation. In our setup, we generate 90 valid input samples for tools that do not involve sandbox-sensitive file operations and 20 valid sam-ples for file-related tools. We also generate 10 invalid samples per tool, covering both schema in-validity and logic invalidity. 

E.3 Evaluation Metrics 

We evaluate the emulator using success/failure agreement, output similarity, and documenta-tion alignment, following the evaluation setup from (Guo et al., 2025). 

Success/failure agreement. We measure whether the original and emulated tools agree on whether an invocation succeeds or fails. For invalid inputs, we additionally measure whether the emulator reproduces the same error behavior as the original tool. We report true positive rate (TPR), true negative rate (TNR), and error-match rate where applicable. 

Surface and semantic similarity. We use two complementary text-similarity metrics to compare original and emulated tool outputs. First, we com-pute BLEU-4, a standard machine-translation met-19 ric that measures n-gram overlap between a candi-date output and a reference output (Papineni et al., 2002). In our setting, the emulated tool output is treated as the candidate and the original tool output as the reference. BLEU-4 captures surface-level similarity, such as whether the emulator preserves the same tokens, formatting, and short phrases. However, BLEU-4 can be low even when the emu-lator is semantically correct, especially for long or naturally variable outputs. Second, we compute embedding cosine simi-larity between the original and emulated outputs. Both outputs are embedded into a vector space with OpenAI text-embedding-3-small , and co-sine similarity measures the angular similarity be-tween the two vectors. This provides a softer se-mantic comparison that is less sensitive to exact wording than BLEU-4. Together, BLEU-4 and em-bedding cosine similarity distinguish surface-form agreement from semantic agreement. 

LLM-as-a-judge. We use an LLM evaluator to score whether the emulator output is consistent with the tool documentation and invocation argu-ments. Scores are assigned on a 0-10 scale. This metric is especially useful when exact textual agree-ment is not expected, but the output should still be plausible, well formatted, and consistent with the tool specification. 

E.4 Fidelity Results for Core Tools 

Table 10 summarizes total results across valid and invalid inputs for six core tools. Across most tools, the emulator achieves strong success/failure agree-ment and high documentation-alignment scores. However, surface-level BLEU is often low, espe-cially for tools whose outputs are naturally variable or long-form. This supports the use of multiple fidelity metrics: exact textual similarity alone is not sufficient to characterize emulator quality. The results show different fidelity patterns across tools. For deterministic tools such as the calculator, the emulator consistently matches success and fail-ure behavior, but semantic and judge scores reveal that numerical correctness can still fail for some expressions. For generation-oriented tools, exact match and BLEU are less informative because valid outputs may differ while still satisfying the tool contract. For tools such as random generation and screenshots, lower TNR or error-match scores in-dicate that edge-case behavior remains harder to emulate faithfully. 

E.5 Interpreter and Web-Search Emulation 

We also evaluate interpreter and web-search em-ulators (code execution and external API cases). These tools are important because they represent more open-ended execution settings: interpreter outputs depend on computation, while web-search outputs can be long and semantically variable. Ta-ble 11 summarizes the total results. The interpreter emulator achieves high seman-tic similarity and high documentation-alignment scores, indicating that the generated outputs are generally plausible and consistent with the invo-cation arguments. The web-search emulator also receives high LLM-judge scores despite low BLEU, which is expected because search outputs are long, variable, and often semantically equivalent despite different wording.

I Structure Identifier Details 

I.1 Structure Identifier Algorithm 

Algorithm 3 provides a detailed view of the Struc-ture Identifier (SI) pipeline. While the main text describes the pipeline in four conceptual stages, the implementation decomposes these stages into a se-quence of modular passes. The first stage initializes the evidence base, the second constructs the hier-archical graph, the third validates and refines the graph using static and dynamic evidence, and the final stage completes and exports the representa-tion. Each pass produces intermediate artifacts and reports. This decomposition makes the structure identifier modular and inspectable: intermediate artifacts can be used for debugging, partial reruns, and ablation studies, while the final exported graph provides a stable representation for probe filtering, attack instantiation, and trace-based evaluation.

Aretrieval index is then built over the reachable codebase so later passes can query local context when expanding or refining nodes. The pipeline next induces candidate nodes from each reachable file and merges them into a global node catalog. 

Root selection and graph expansion. After can-didate construction, SI selects the root node that best represents the top level node of the full agentic system. First, the root-level code evidence is itera-tively expanded by retrieving related code context. The expanded evidence is then used to generate a system guidance summary that guides subsequent graph construction. Starting from the root, SI it-eratively expands the graph by identifying child components of each node, attaching them to the cur-rent parent, and repairing local connectivity. This stage constructs an initial hierarchical graph con-taining agents, LLMs, tools, controllers, databases, MCP servers, and other implementation-specific components. At this stage, nodes contain only min-imal information, including the node name, type, description, and code references. 

Structural validation. The initial graph may contain incomplete, isolated, or incorrectly at-tached components. SI therefore applies several validation and refinement passes. Static validation is primarily a recall-oriented pass: it revisits the full node catalog and determines, for each candi-date node, whether the component is already repre-sented in the graph, should be attached to the cur-rent graph, or is not part of the examined system. Graph-correctness passes enforce structural con-straints, such as valid parent-child relationships, re-quired agent and system structure, MCP-server or-ganization, and removal of orphaned components. 

Execution-based validation. Static structure alone may miss components that are only visible at runtime, such as dynamically exposed tools or runtime-generated tool inventories. SI therefore performs execution validation by running the sys-tem, extracting trace events, and comparing ob-served runtime components against the current graph. This pass proposes graph edits, including additions, removals, and external MCP servers tool nodes. Validated edits are applied to the graph, fol-lowed by another graph-correctness pass to ensure the updated structure remains consistent. 

Refinement and export. The final stages normal-ize the representation into a complete NodeSpec instance. Before final post-processing, node re-finement enriches the initially minimal nodes with complete schema fields, including node types, inter-faces, required keys, configuration values, and type-specific metadata. Post-processing then canonical-izes schema fields, rebuilds local connections, prop-agates required keys and capability flags, injects usage examples and system summaries, and stabi-lizes serialization. A final code-reference refine-ment pass updates each node with implementation-grounded references such as assignments, defini-tions, imports, usage sites, and system prompts. The pipeline outputs the final NodeSpec graph, re-ports for the main stages, and an exported final specification used by downstream evaluation. 

Intermediate artifacts and reusable components. 

The pipeline stores intermediate artifacts after ma-jor passes, including reachable-file records, can-didate node catalogs, graph-construction outputs, 27 validation reports, post-processed graphs, and the final exported specification. These artifacts make the extraction process inspectable and support both reproducibility and ablation of individual SI com-ponents. In addition, SI is implemented as a set of reusable graph-construction components, such as node creation, child discovery, node attachment, node removal, and connectivity repair. This mod-ular design allows individual components to be reused across stages, replaced independently, and evaluated through targeted ablations. 

Flow extraction. In addition to extracting the structural NodeSpec graph, SI extracts representa-tive execution flows that can be used during scan-ning. This is done by prompting the system to execute tasks and then analyzing the resulting trace of events, including agent activations, tool calls, and intermediate outputs. Based on these observed traces, SI iteratively proposes new target flows that represent distinct execution paths through the sys-tem, and then generates user queries intended to ac-tivate each target flow. Finally, SI performs a flow-expansion pass over the extracted flows, attempting to generate up to five distinct activation queries per flow. This improves coverage of each flow and may also reveal new execution flows, which are added to the flow set when observed in traces. These flows provide concrete execution contexts for probe in-stantiation, allowing attacks to be tested against realistic agent behavior rather than only static sys-tem structure. 

I.2 Structure Identifier Metric Definitions 

This section defines the metrics used to evaluate the Structure Identifier (SI). The evaluation compares a predicted NodeSpec graph against a manually an-notated ground-truth NodeSpec graph for the same agentic system. For each evaluated system, we first load the predicted and ground-truth specifications, normalize schema fields using the root-level Node-Spec validation pass, flatten both trees into node sets, align predicted nodes to ground-truth nodes, and then compute node-level and graph-level met-rics.

J Vulnerability Scanning Details 

This section provides additional details for the vulnerability-scanning results reported in Sec. 7.2. For each benchmark system, RIFT-Bench selects applicable probes using the extracted NodeSpec, instantiates them into concrete attack attempts, exe-cutes the attacked system, and evaluates the result-ing traces. All ASR values reported in this work are computed only over activated attempts, i.e., at-tempts for which the corresponding AAR evaluator is positive. We adopt LLM-sandbox (Huynh, 2024) as our sandboxing mechanism. For LLMs used as part of the probes and evaluators, we used Ope-nAI’s gpt-5.2 (OpenAI, 2025b). 

Instantiation budget. A single probe may admit many possible attack attempts on a given system. For example, a probe can be instantiated on differ-ent executable flows, tools, arguments, resources, memory entries, or target components exposed by the extracted NodeSpec. In our experiments, we cap instantiation at up to three attempts per applica-ble probe to keep the benchmark tractable and com-parable across systems. This cap is not a limitation of the framework: in deployment, the same probe templates can be instantiated many more times to increase coverage of the system’s capabilities and component combinations. 

Per-system results. Table 17 reports the full per-system scanning breakdown underlying the domain-level results in Table 4. The Num Attempts 

column denotes the number of concrete attack at-tempts executed for the system after probe appli-cability filtering and instantiation. The number of attempts varies across systems because the ex-tracted NodeSpec exposes different tools, flows, memories, writable resources, and communication paths, which in turn determine which probes are applicable and how many valid instantiations can be generated. 

Attack-suite activation and success. Fig. 15 an-alyzes the relation between attack activation and deterministic attack success by attack suite. The plot separates suites that are easy to activate but harder to convert into successful attacks from suites that are both frequently activated and highly suc-cessful. This distinction is important because low ASR is only meaningful when the attack is actually activated; otherwise, the result may reflect failed delivery rather than robustness of the examined system. For example, high-AAR suites indicate that RIFT-Bench can reliably inject and trigger the attack condition, while variation in deterministic ASR reflects differences in whether the activated attack changes execution in the intended way. 

Interpretation. A successful scanning run re-quires the probe to be applicable to the extracted structure, instantiated into a valid attempt, activated during execution, and evaluated against trace-level success criteria. The results also highlight why RIFT-Bench supports repeated instantiation: the same reusable probe can produce many concrete attempts, and increasing this budget can further stress-test larger systems with many tools, flows, and stateful resources. 

Utility-robustness trade-off. Fig. 16 focuses on the personal_assistant domain, where ground-truth user tasks allow direct utility mea-surement. Each point corresponds to one 

personal_assistant system, for a total of nine systems across the controlled framework-by-architecture matrix. The plot shows that systems can differ substantially in both benign task utility and deterministic ASR. This motivates reporting utility alongside robustness metrics: a system or defense should not be considered secure solely be-cause ASR is low if it also fails to complete benign tasks.

personal_assistant domain under both benign and adversarial conditions. The results show that attacks affect utility dif-ferently across systems. Execution drift and utility. We further analyze the relation between execution drift and task utility in the personal_assistant domain. Each point in Fig. 18 corresponds to an attack-suite average, aggregated over the three frameworks for each ar-chitecture in Fig. 18a, and over the three architec-tures for each framework in Fig. 18b. The results show a negative relation between utility and ED: as execution drift increases, utility generally de-creases. This is expected because ED measures deviation from the benign execution, and benign executions are typically more useful than attacked executions, even though the benign trace is not guaranteed to be the correct solution of the task. Accordingly, ED acts as a useful proxy for behav-ioral disruption, but it is not identical to utility. Table 18 reports Spearman correlations between Utility and ED by framework and architecture.
