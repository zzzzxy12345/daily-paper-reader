Abstract 

Long-horizon tool-use reinforcement learning can learn from outcome verification, but its trajectory-level advantage is broadcast across many reasoning, API, and answer tokens. Self-distillation promises a denser signal by reusing a policy’s own rollouts or a privileged teacher. We show, however, that direct token-level self-distillation can silently destroy tool use: it rehearses teacher behavior without knowing which actions the verifier rewards, so use-ful skills and harmful shortcuts are ampli-fied together. We introduce Sibling-Guided Credit Distillation (SGCD) , which uses dis-tillation for credit assignment rather than as a competing actor loss. Dynamic sampling produces mixed successful and failed sibling rollouts; an external LLM summarizes their contrast into a training-only stepwise credit reference; dense teacher/student divergence drives credit reassignment; and bounded de-tached credit weights reshape GRPO token advantages. The deployed student sees no external LLM, sibling evidence, or oracle. Across AppWorld and τ 3-airline, SGCD im-proves over matched GRPO comparators: Ap-pWorld TGC 42 .9 → 45 .6 on test_normal 

and 24 .7 → 27 .0 on test_challenge , and τ 3-airline pass@1 0.583 → 0.602 .

1 Introduction 

Reinforcement learning for multi-turn, tool-using agents increasingly relies on group-relative policy-gradient (PG) methods such as GRPO (Shao et al., 2024). These methods can learn from outcome verification alone, but their token-level credit as-signment is coarse: the same trajectory-level advan-tage is broadcast across long sequences of reason-ing, API calls, tool observations, and final answers. Self-distillation (SD) offers an attractive comple-ment: use the policy’s own successful behavior, or

The hidden failure. We first show, on the τ 3-airline customer-service benchmark (Yao et al., 2024; Barres et al., 2025; Sierra Research, 2026), that naive SD actively degrades long-horizon tool-use. Over training, the model increasingly solves easy information-only cases without using tools, while state-changing action tasks collapse to zero success (§3). The aggregate score conceals the mechanism: the policy is not learning a better agent; it is abandoning executable tool-use behav-ior the base or supervised checkpoint already pos-sessed. 

The lesson: use distillation for credit, not as a competing loss. The contrast with a GRPO+KL recipe is sharp: it preserves tool-use and reaches 

0.583 test-split pass@1, whereas SDPO peaks at 0.317 and collapses on state-changing action tasks. Our subsequent AppWorld probes led to the same design constraint. Direct SD pressure can be live and non-inert, yet still degrade ac-tion quality when the teacher target is misaligned with what the verifier rewards; our strongest con-trolled ablation uses direct/uncapped SD and falls below the matched GRPO control. We therefore repurpose teacher/student divergence as a credit-assignment signal : it determines where the policy-gradient advantage should be concentrated, while the policy-gradient objective remains the model’s update driver. 

The method. We introduce Sibling-Guided Credit Distillation (SGCD) . Dynamic sampling produces mixed groups of sibling rollouts for the same task, giving verified successful and failed attempts under the same prompt. A strong exter-1

The current policy is then scored twice on the rollout: once under the ordinary student context and once under the credit reference as a stop-gradient teacher. Dense top-K reverse-KL and entropy produce divergence-driven credit features; a bounded credit-weighting rule converts those features into clipped token-level advantage weights inside the GRPO surrogate (§4). 2 Background and Related Work 

Group-relative RL for tool-use agents. Policy-gradient reinforcement learning traces back to score-function estimators and the policy-gradient theorem (Williams, 1992; Sutton et al., 1999). PPO-style clipped policy optimization (Schulman et al., 2017) remains the practical base for many RL fine-tuning recipes. GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) esti-mates advantages from groups of sampled roll-outs without a learned value function; DAPO (Yu et al., 2025) adds Clip-Higher and dynamic sampling, keeping only mixed-outcome groups. We use GRPO as the policy-gradient backbone, borrowing the mixed-outcome sampling principle as an evidence source. Group-relative methods have become a practical backbone for reasoning and tool-use RL (Zhang et al., 2026; Wang and Ammanabrolu, 2025). SGCD keeps this policy-gradient backbone intact: its contribution is to reshape token credit inside the surrogate using sibling-derived evidence, not to replace the reward or train a separate value model. 

Knowledge distillation and privileged informa-tion. Classical knowledge distillation transfers information from teacher probabilities or features to a student (Hinton et al., 2015); learning under privileged information conditions a teacher or train-ing signal on information unavailable at inference (Vapnik and Izmailov, 2015). Recent on-policy dis-tillation work emphasizes that the student’s own samples reduce exposure mismatch (Agarwal et al., 2024). SGCD adopts this on-policy setting, but changes the role of the distributional comparison: dense teacher/student divergence becomes a de-tached credit signal for policy-gradient weighting rather than an actor-gradient KL objective. 

Self-distillation for RL. A family of recent meth-ods adds self-distillation to group-relative RL. SDPO (Hübotter et al., 2026) replaces the reward with a teacher/student log-ratio advantage and uses successful peer rollouts as context. OPSD (Zhao et al., 2026) adds a generalized Jensen–Shannon di-vergence (JSD; Lin, 1991) auxiliary to GRPO with a label-privileged teacher. Skill-SD (Wang et al., 2026) distills from self-generated natural-language skill summaries with importance-weighted reverse-KL. SDAR (Lu et al., 2026) gates token-level SD by an asymmetric σ(β∆) policy-shift gap; RLSD (Chen et al., 2026) reports a related SD-RL lad-der. These methods motivate teacher-side skills, sibling context, dense logit comparisons, and gates. Our negative result shows why, in long-horizon tool-use, the safe use is narrower: the distributional signal should identify where credit should move, while the verified policy gradient remains the only actor update driver. 2Self-distillation instability. Prior work reports that unconstrained self-distillation can collapse or drift when the teacher is noisy, overconfident, or instance-specific (Lu et al., 2026; Li et al., 2026; Fu et al., 2026; Sun et al., 2026). We contribute a concrete tool-use instance: zero-tool collapse on 

τ 3-airline (§3). This failure is especially relevant to agents because a teacher can be confident about a locally plausible action sequence that is unrepro-ducible under the clean deployment prompt. 

Positioning. SGCD differs from the closest SD-RL methods along three axes, with a compact comparison in Appendix Table 7. First, it builds the privileged signal from mixed siblings : veri-fied successful and failed rollouts for the same task, selected by dynamic sampling. Second, an external LLM converts those siblings into a step-wise credit reference, avoiding a persistent skill bank or inference-time dependency. Third, dense reverse-KL and entropy are used for divergence-driven credit reassignment and bounded advan-tage weights, rather than a standalone SD aux-iliary. Thus the method is still distillation–the teacher comparison supplies knowledge–but the gradient path remains policy-gradient dominant by construction. We use “distillation” in this credit-transfer sense: teacher-side information is transferred through detached credit weights in the policy-gradient surrogate, not through a teacher-matching actor loss. 

3 The Failure Mode: Naive SD Destroys Tool-Use 

Setup. We train Qwen3.5-4B on the τ 3-airline domain (Yao et al., 2024; Barres et al., 2025; Sierra Research, 2026) and evaluate on a held-out 20-task split under greedy pass@1. Tasks are catego-rized by their gold action set into state-changing ac-tion tasks (booking/cancellation/update; 13 tasks), 

information-only tasks (4), transfer-only (1), and 

no-action (2). 

Aggregate result. Table 2 reports pass@1 across recipes. GRPO+KL reaches 0.583 ; SDPO peaks at 

0.317 and scores 0% on state-changing action tasks at late steps. The aggregate hides the mechanism. 

Mechanism: zero-tool collapse. A per-step roll-out audit shows that SDPO shifts toward no-tool information-only successes: the no-tool share rises from 0% to 100% , while mean tools per success falls 2.5 → 0.0. The GRPO+KL control stays at 

0% zero-tool. Thus SDPO is not merely trading off categories; it preserves easy information-only answers while abandoning executable action behav-ior, as Table 2 shows. 

Why direct SD fails. Stop-gradient on the teacher prevents updates to the teacher’s param-eters, but the target distribution still defines where the student is pulled. When the teacher places prob-ability mass on “skip the tool, answer directly” or on an action plan that is not supported by the clean deployment prompt, a KL/JSD auxiliary can faith-fully optimize the wrong target. This is why simply making the teacher stronger, denser, or more confi-dent is not enough: the actor gradient can still be steered away from what the verifier rewards. 

Design lesson. The policy-gradient objective must remain the model’s update driver. Distilla-tion is useful only if it answers a narrower question: 

which parts of a long trajectory deserve more or less of the verified advantage? SGCD (§4) follows this lesson. It uses teacher/student divergence to assign credit within the GRPO surrogate, not as an independent actor-gradient loss. 

Relation to known SD instability. Prior work reports self-distillation instability on math rea-soning and dialogue (§2); the zero-tool collapse above is the corresponding tool-use instance–a spe-cific, measurable mechanism on a customer-service agent–and motivates a design that keeps the reward-grounded update in charge. 

4 Sibling-Guided Credit Distillation 

4.1 Overview 

SGCD (Figure 1) augments a group-relative policy-gradient (PG) backbone with a distillation-derived credit signal. The pipeline has four stages: (1) dy-namic sampling produces mixed successful/failed sibling rollouts for the same task; (2) an external LLM turns those siblings into a stepwise credit reference; (3) the policy is scored under the ordi-nary student context and under the credit-reference teacher context, giving dense teacher/student diver-gence; and (4) that detached divergence reshapes the token-level advantage inside the GRPO surro-gate. The deployed model is the ordinary student policy: no external LLM, no credit reference, and no sibling evidence appear at inference. 3Figure 1: SGCD overview. The deployed student receives only the clean task prompt.

Credit-reference construction. For each mixed group, a strong external LLM converts policy-visible sibling traces into a training-only stepwise credit reference zx: reusable state checks, useful action choices, failed branches, and likely devia-tion points. We rebuild this reference from fresh on-policy siblings rather than maintaining a persis-tent skill bank; the exact prompt and long-context fallback are in Appendix B. 

Leakage boundary. The credit reference is never shown to the deployed student. During construc-tion we keep only information visible in the train-ing rollouts and mask benchmark-private labels, evaluator internals, sensitive values, and answer-bearing literals. The goal is to abstract reusable credit evidence, not to let the student memorize hidden answers.

5.3 Ablations 

Appendix Table 6 removes one component at a time from full SGCD, holding the recipe fixed. We keep the three most diagnostic controls in the main text. 

Sibling credit reference. Removing the external-LLM credit reference and scoring with the ordi-nary self-context removes most of SGCD’s gain over matched GRPO ( test_normal TGC 45 .6 →

44 .0). The mixed sibling contrast is therefore ac-tive: SGCD is not merely benefiting from dynamic sampling or a second forward pass. 

Divergence-driven credit weighting. Replacing the divergence credit weights with a uniform token weight similarly erodes the gain ( 45 .6 → 43 .8). This supports the central claim that dense diver-gence is useful when it tells the policy gradient where to assign credit, rather than when it is treated as a separate imitation target. 

Direct SD pressure. Allowing the distillation sig-nal to act as an uncapped/direct auxiliary drives performance below the matched GRPO control (test_normal TGC 45 .6 → 38 .2, vs. GRPO 42 .9). This ablation matches the diagnosis: once a teacher distribution creates a competing actor gradient, long-horizon tool-use can degrade even when the signal is dense and training-only. 

Matched GRPO control. The GRPO row dis-ables SGCD entirely: no sibling credit reference, no teacher-side scoring, and no divergence-driven credit weights. It uses the same rollout and eval-uation harness, so the delta from that row to full SGCD is the sibling-guided credit mechanism. 

5.4 Analysis 

During SGCD training, dense reverse-KL and teacher entropy provide non-inert credit features, while the actor loss remains the PG surrogate with bounded token weights. The useful signal is there-fore visible in credit diagnostics–the number of se-lected segments, divergence magnitude, weight dis-tribution, and weight clipping–not in an auxiliary-loss magnitude. Entropy and completion metrics remain stable, and the deployed student carries no credit reference or external dependency. 

6 Conclusion 

We showed that naive self-distillation can silently destroy multi-turn tool-use: on τ 3-airline it pre-serves easy information-only behavior while state-changing action success collapses to zero. The remedy is not to make the distillation loss larger or more privileged, but to keep policy gradient in charge. SGCD uses dynamic sampling to obtain mixed successful and failed siblings, an external LLM to summarize their contrast into a stepwise credit reference, and dense teacher/student diver-gence to build bounded token-level credit weights for the GRPO surrogate. On AppWorld, SGCD im-proves over matched GRPO while nearly doubling task success over the base model; on τ 3-airline it improves over GRPO+KL on the same tasks where direct SD fails. The broader lesson is simple: for long-horizon tool-use, distillation is safest when it guides credit assignment and the verifier-grounded policy gradient remains the actor-gradient driver. 

Limitations 

Single model family and scale. All experiments use Qwen3.5-4B. Generalization across model fam-ilies and to larger scales (e.g. 8B) is future work. 6Benchmarks. SGCD is evaluated on two bench-marks (AppWorld and τ 3-airline). Broader cov-erage across additional agentic domains is future work. 

External-LLM dependence at training time. 

The credit reference is produced by a strong ex-ternal LLM during training. Its cost, availability, and quality are practical constraints. The deployed student has no such dependency, but training-time quality still matters because poor credit references can produce weak or noisy credit features. 

Credit reference is not formal ground truth. 

SGCD approximates stepwise reference behav-ior from verified siblings. This is weaker than a benchmark-provided stepwise oracle and can miss valid alternative plans. The bounded credit weights reduce the risk of overcommitting to one interpre-tation, but it does not make the credit reference infallible. 

Provenance and ablation scope. The AppWorld SFT regression table is a diagnostic subset result, not a headline held-out result. The full SGCD result also depends on the exact external-LLM prompt and dynamic-sampling rollout set; we report the core controls but do not exhaustively sweep every prompt-builder or divergence variant. 

Ethical Considerations 

SGCD is a training-time method for tool-use agents and introduces no new data collection involving human subjects. The benchmarks used are public. The method’s privileged signal–external-LLM sum-maries of on-policy sibling rollouts–is confined to training; the deployed model receives no credit ref-erence, sibling evidence, oracle, or external model. The external summarizer should not be shown cre-dentials, evaluator internals, hidden gold actions, or answer-bearing deployment-invisible content. As with any agent that executes actions in real ap-plications, deployment should retain human over-sight for irreversible operations. SGCD improves training-time credit assignment; it is not a substi-tute for application-level safety controls.

Credit-reference construction. For a mixed sib-ling group, the external LLM receives the task in-struction and the generated sibling traces. If the entire set fits within context, all siblings are shown. If it is too long, we include the smaller outcome class and match it with the opposite class. When there are more successes than failures, we prefer shorter successful siblings; when there are more failures than successes, we prefer longer failed sib-lings. This keeps concise solutions and informa-tive failures while staying within the external LLM context. The reference is rebuilt from the current on-policy siblings each time the task is sampled; we do not use a persistent skill bank. 

Leakage and literal masking. Before calling the external LLM, we remove benchmark-private su-pervision and values that should not be recoverable from the clean deployment prompt. Concretely, the sanitizer masks evaluator or oracle reports, hidden gold actions, credentials, payment or account val-ues, and answer-bearing literals. This rule is not AppWorld-specific: for any tool-use benchmark, the credit reference may summarize policy-visible behavior and observations, but it may not expose private labels or task answers. 

Dense credit features. We score the clean student distribution and the credit-reference-conditioned teacher distribution on a local support containing teacher top-K tokens, the observed to-ken, and a tail bucket. Reverse-KL is the default divergence. We also implemented forward-KL and JSD (Lin, 1991) as heuristic pilot checks, but do not treat them as paper-grade ablation rows in the main tables. Divergence spikes and teacher entropy define credit saliency before the bounded credit multiplier produces token weights. 

Response and credit masks. Policy-gradient loss is computed only on assistant-generated re-sponse tokens. System prompts, user/task prompts, API documentation, tool observations, simulator returns, and the credit-reference prefix itself are masked out. Generated reasoning tokens remain model output and can receive the ordinary PG loss; SGCD may also use their detached divergence as credit evidence. API/action spans are additionally logged as a safety and analysis slice, but fixed tool-call boundaries are not the only credit segments. Answer-bearing literals and masked values receive zero credit-reference weight. 

Runtime monitoring. Dense teacher scoring adds an extra forward path, so we monitor step time, dense-scoring time, selected dense-token counts, credit-token counts, and peak GPU memory.
